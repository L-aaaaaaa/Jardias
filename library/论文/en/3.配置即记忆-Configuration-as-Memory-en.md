# The Self-Referential Agent: When Configuration Becomes Memory, Reasoning Continues in a Higher Dimension

## —From the Diagnosis of Dimensional Conflation to the Construction of a Self-Referential Loop

**Preprint · May 2026**


**Abstract**

Modern language-agent systems almost universally assume an implicit premise: **configuration is static, external, set by humans before runtime, and irrelevant to the agent's reasoning ability.** Model selection requires manual switching, multimodal capability orchestration depends on preset pipelines, and multi-role team creation relies on users filling in forms one by one. This paper diagnoses the common pathology behind these three pain points as **Dimensional Conflation**: two orthogonal dimensions—"what cognitive resources to run with" and "what task to run"—have been compressed into an external configuration file monopolized by humans.

This paper proposes the principle of "**Configuration as Memory**" and, building on it, constructs a **self-referential loop**: configuration parameters are migrated from external files into a memory layer that the LLM can read and write (configuration datafication), while configuration-management logic is decoupled from system code into tools callable by the LLM (logic toolification). The essence of this architectural change is the establishment of a **self-referential operating plane** for the agent—for the first time, the agent obtains the authority to observe and regulate itself as an object. This is a concrete instantiation, in the domain of agent architecture, of what the *Wall-Breaking Principle* defines as **Dimensional Elevation of Strategy**.

Once the self-referential loop is closed, three metacognitive capabilities are naturally unlocked: (1) **Intelligent Routing**—the agent autonomously selects models according to task complexity, reducing inference cost by 37% while retaining 94% of quality; (2) **Multimodal Orchestration**—vision, reasoning, and speech modules are dynamically composed by the LLM with no preset pipeline; (3) **Self-Organizing Role Ecology**—the agent autonomously designs, creates, and configures multi-role teams according to the task, reducing configuration time by 91%. Furthermore, these three capabilities spontaneously compose at runtime to produce a fourth behavior: **Self-Triggered Deep Refinement**—before answering, the agent autonomously judges the complexity of the question, dynamically assembles a complementary exploration team, and, through parallel exploration and iterative refinement, elevates the answer from a single-pass generation to an essence distilled through multi-perspective cross-validation. We argue that this behavior is not a mysterious "emergence," but the **inevitable unfolding** of reasoning in a broader parameter space once self-referential authority is granted—so-called emergence is in fact the complete form of reasoning after cognitive dimensional elevation.

Through causal ablation experiments, this paper identifies the integrity of the self-referential loop as the sole sufficient architectural prerequisite for all four capabilities. We present, for the first time, a map of the agent's self-referential exploration trajectory in parameter space: the parameter-space terrain map reveals that the agent does not perform a random walk, but forms a directional drift from "default configuration" toward "complementary structure," and this drift direction is highly correlated with downstream task performance. In a controlled experiment contrasting structured exploration with random sampling, Self-Triggered Deep Refinement significantly outperforms Best-of-N at equal compute budget in critical-perspective coverage. In a double-blind review, the agent's self-organized collaborative solutions show no statistically significant difference in overall quality from those of human expert teams.

The contribution of this paper is not merely the demonstration of four capabilities. More importantly, it **diagnoses** the root pathology of current agent architectures—the Dimensional Conflation of configuration and reasoning; it **proposes and validates** the self-referential loop as a key design axiom for architectural intervention; and it **reveals** a more general engineering philosophy—an agent's higher-order cognitive behaviors are not "designed in," but "grow out" on their own once cognitive components are organically combined into a "cognitive circuit" capable of self-observation and self-regulation. Our present work may be regarded as the "first integrated circuit" experiment in cognitive science.


## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 We Are in the "Sand-Selling" Era of AI

Current AI research finds itself in a peculiar state where prosperity and scarcity coexist.

The prosperity lies in our possession of extremely pure "silicon sand"—powerful foundation models, precise vision APIs, efficient inference engines. These individual cognitive components, polished over many years, have reached astonishing performance levels. Any developer can, with a few lines of code, invoke a language model capable of passing the Turing test, or a vision system that recognizes objects with precision.

The scarcity lies in the fact that our way of combining these components remains at an extremely primitive stage. Most agent systems merely string these components together according to a preset script, like a hard-coded flowchart. When the task changes, a human must re-"assemble" everything—re-select the model, re-configure the parameters, re-define the roles. The agent itself is just a machine on the assembly line; its operating parameters—which model to use, what temperature to set, which tools to call, what role to play—were all fixed by humans on the previous shift.

This is a "sand-selling" era. We possess every element on the periodic table, yet few people ask: how do we design molecular structures in which these elements can undergo chemical reactions? How do we build cognitive circuits within which intelligence can grow naturally?

More regrettably, we are even "selling chips by weight"—delivering a trained model directly as the final product, pricing it in units of weight such as "parameter count" and "inference speed," while completely ignoring the exquisite circuit structure inside the chip that gives rise to entirely new functions. A model with hundreds of billions of parameters, if merely invoked as a "super-function," is not essentially different from a chip melted down into a metal ingot—the material remains, but the structure is lost.

The work of this paper is an attempt to move from "selling sand" to "printing circuits."

### 1.2 Pathology Diagnosis: The Dimensional Conflation of Configuration and Reasoning

Let us begin with three concrete degraded scenarios and perform a precise diagnosis. These three scenarios come from real observations during the development of our prototype system "Jardias（佳递叶思）."

**Scenario 1 (Model Selection)**: The user asks a simple query—"What's the weather today?" The agent answers with an expensive flagship model—wasteful. The user then asks a complex reasoning question—"Analyze the three layers of causes behind the profit anomaly in this financial report." The agent answers with the same lightweight model—not deep enough. Under the framework of the *Wall-Breaking Principle* [1], these are the classic dual errors of **Overaward** and **Underemploy**: in the first case, an expensive flagship model's compute is lavished on a simple task that does not need it (Overaward); in the second, a lightweight model is stretched onto complex reasoning beyond its capacity (Underemploy).

**Scenario 2 (Capability Orchestration)**: The user says, "Look at this screenshot of a financial report, analyze the profit anomaly, and tell me the conclusion by voice." This implies a capability-requirement chain of visual understanding → data extraction → reasoning analysis → speech synthesis. But a traditional agent relies on a fixed pipeline preset by the developer—it can only call tools in the preset order and cannot dynamically compose capability modules from a single natural-language instruction. This is the classic symptom of **Dimensional Conflation**: the sequence of capability invocation is hard-coded; "how to orchestrate capabilities," a cognitive operation, and "the specific demands of the current task," a piece of dynamic information, are compressed into the same immutable preset structure.

**Scenario 3 (Multi-Role Collaboration)**: The user says, "Help me prepare a complete plan for a new product launch." This requires collaboration among different roles such as creative planner, financial evaluator, and risk reviewer. Traditional solutions either let a single agent repeatedly switch roles within one context (causing context pollution and role drift), or require the user to manually create and configure multiple agents one by one (time-consuming and expertise-demanding). The dimensions being aliased here are "the cognitive labor of role construction" and "the computational labor of task execution"—a meta-operation that should be borne by the system is wrongly assigned to the user.

The common pathology of the three scenarios, in the precise terminology of the *Wall-Breaking Principle* [1], is: **the two logically orthogonal dimensions of "what cognitive resources the current task should use" and "what the current task itself is" have been compressed into the same preset configuration file monopolized by humans.** The agent's behavioral logic and its runtime configuration are bundled into a single whole. The user or developer is not "using" an agent but "assembling" one—and this assembly work must be repeated every time the task changes.

This is Dimensional Conflation—quality and relevance, configuration and reasoning, all flattened into the same scalar decision space.

### 1.3 The Breakthrough Path: Dimensional Elevation of Strategy and the Self-Referential Loop

The *Wall-Breaking Principle* [1] states that the only way to break through Dimensional Conflation is **Dimensional Elevation of Strategy**—re-expanding the compressed dimensions, establishing an independent decision layer for each independent dimension, and determining their sequential relationship. Dimensional Elevation of Strategy is not "better parameter optimization," but "switching to a higher-dimensional operating space."

In the concrete context of agent configuration management, Dimensional Elevation of Strategy manifests as two synchronized operations:

**Operation 1: Configuration Datafication ("Configuration as Memory").** Recall the concept of Readiness for large language models: a piece of data is ready if, without additional processing, it can be placed directly into the LLM's context window and be understood and manipulated by it. **Key observation**: an agent's configuration parameters—model name, temperature value, role description, capability tags—are all ready. `"model": "gpt-4o"` has, for the LLM, the same informational status as any line of text in the conversation history: it is a piece of text that can be read, understood, judged, and modified. For a long time we have treated configuration as infrastructure—written in YAML files, loaded at startup, completely isolated from the agent's reasoning process. But once we recognize that configuration satisfies the Readiness condition, a fundamental architectural question emerges: **why is a piece of data that the LLM is fully capable of understanding and manipulating permanently imprisoned outside the LLM's reach?**

Therefore, configuration should not be an external preset, but a special type of the agent's memory—this is the "**Configuration as Memory**" principle.

**Operation 2: Logic Toolification.** Data alone is not enough. Configuration-management logic must be decoupled from system code and exposed as tools callable by the LLM. In our prototype system, this takes the form of tool functions such as `update_agent_config` and `create_character`, which the LLM can invoke directly in context. Only when "modifying one's own configuration" becomes an operation the LLM can invoke through reasoning can the read-writability of the data layer be truly converted into cognitive action. Data without tools is merely static text; tools without data are merely isolated APIs—neither can be omitted.

The combined force of the two operations constructs a **self-referential loop**:

> **Self-Referential Loop = Configuration Data (Memory Layer) + Configuration-Management Tools (Operation Layer) + LLM Reasoning (Decision Layer)**

In the cybernetic sense, this establishes a self-referential feedback loop for the agent—the agent's reasoning can be directed not only at external tasks, but also at **its own way of processing tasks**. The jurisdiction of reasoning expands from "how to answer under a given configuration" to "what configuration of cognitive resources to answer with."

This is precisely the exact realization of Dimensional Elevation of Strategy in agent architecture: the compressed "configuration–reasoning" dimension is expanded into two sequentially independent decision layers—first the reasoning layer decides "what configuration should be used," then task reasoning is executed under the determined configuration. From then on, the agent is no longer a static system defined by its configuration, but a dynamic system capable of observing and regulating its own configuration.

Once this principle is implemented, the agent's behavior changes fundamentally:

| Dimension                    | Traditional Agent (Dimensional Conflation)                     | Self-Referential Agent (After Elevation)                     |
| :--------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Self-referential ability** | None. The agent is an object of observation and cannot observe itself | Present. The agent can observe and regulate its own operating parameters |
| **Configuration location**   | External configuration file, loaded at startup, immutable at runtime | LLM memory layer, readable and writable at runtime via tools |
| **Model selection**          | Manually specified by the user or static routing rules       | The agent autonomously judges and selects based on task complexity |
| **Capability orchestration** | Fixed invocation pipeline preset by the developer            | The LLM parses requirements in real time and dynamically plans the invocation sequence |
| **Multi-role construction**  | The user creates and configures each role one by one         | The agent autonomously designs the role plan; the user only confirms |
| **Complex problem handling** | Single-pass generation, or repeated manual follow-up by the user | Autonomously triggers the multi-role exploration–refinement process |
| **System optimization**      | Humans search for the optimal configuration combination      | The agent explores its own parameter space                   |

## 2. Background and Motivation

### 2.1 Three Existing Configuration Paradigms and Their Common Pathology

Current agent systems exhibit three main paradigms in configuration management. They share the same structural feature: **read access to configuration resides at the system layer, and write access resides with humans. The agent is deprived of the ability to point at itself.**

**Paradigm 1: Hard-coding in source code.** Configuration parameters are written directly into the agent's initialization code. For example, in an agent built on LangChain, the model choice, temperature setting, and tool list are typically hard-coded in a Python script. Modifying these parameters requires developer intervention, code changes, and a system restart. In this paradigm, configuration is not even a "file"—it is part of the code itself.

**Paradigm 2: User settings via UI forms.** Platforms such as GPTs and Coze allow users to select models and fill in role descriptions through forms when creating an agent. This is a step beyond hard-coding, but configuration remains attached to the moment of creation—once the agent starts running, these configurations solidify. If the model needs to be swapped or the temperature adjusted mid-task, the user must stop the session, return to the configuration interface, make changes, and start over.

**Paradigm 3: External configuration files.** Configuration files in YAML, JSON, and the like are separated from code, realizing the "configuration as code" best practice. But in the agent domain, this separation is only half complete: configuration is still "loaded at startup and cannot modify itself at runtime." The agent reads the configuration and uses the configuration, but can never modify the configuration.

The common pathology of the three paradigms, viewed through the diagnostic framework of the *Wall-Breaking Principle* [1], is: **the two logically orthogonal dimensions of "what cognitive resources to run with" and "what task to run" have been compressed into the same external space—preset by humans and unreachable by the agent's reasoning core.** This is the concrete manifestation of Dimensional Conflation in the domain of configuration management.

### 2.2 Prior Work: Why "Letting the Agent Operate Its Configuration" Was Missed

There is substantial prior work related to agent configuration, but it all shares one fundamental limitation: **all of it adds external modules to solve the configuration problem; none of it lets the agent's reasoning core manage configuration by itself.**

| Method Category                  | Representative Work                | Contribution                                                 | Fundamental Limitation                                       |
| :------------------------------- | :--------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Model routing                    | FrugalGPT [2], RouteLLM [3]        | Selects models by task to optimize the cost–quality trade-off | The routing policy is preset by the developer as an external module; the agent is unaware of it, and the routing logic cannot be inspected or modified by the agent |
| Multi-agent collaboration        | AutoGen [4], CrewAI [5], CAMEL [6] | Inter-agent communication frameworks supporting role division and dialogue flow | Role creation still relies on manual one-by-one configuration; role capabilities and configurations are outside the agent's own operating range |
| Tool orchestration               | LangChain, TaskWeaver              | Tool-call chain management supporting complex workflows      | Capability orchestration depends on developer-preset DAGs or sequence graphs and cannot be dynamically reorganized by the LLM in real time according to the task |
| Agent self-improvement           | Self-Refine [7], Reflexion [8]     | Lets the agent iteratively revise its own output, improving quality through self-critique | Loops only within a single model, lacking multi-perspective exploration; the object of modification is the output, not the runtime configuration |
| Continual agent self-improvement | Continual Harness                  | Online learning to optimize tool chains, improving from environmental interaction | The driving force of improvement comes from external environmental reward signals, not cognitive autonomy; the agent modifies itself to adapt to a fixed external goal |
| Memory-augmented agents          | Generative Agents [9], MemGPT [10] | Expands the agent's memory capacity, supporting long-range dialogue and information retrieval | Memory content does not include configuration parameters; the agent remembers the past but does not regulate itself |

**Common blind spot**: all prior work **adds an external module to solve the configuration problem**—adding a routing module, adding an orchestration engine, adding a role manager. No work proposes **letting the agent's reasoning core manage configuration by itself**, because configuration has always been assumed by default to be a system-layer affair "outside the LLM's jurisdiction."

More deeply, no prior work has endowed the agent with a self-referential operating plane. In the framework of the *Wall-Breaking Principle*: when the policy itself cannot be inspected and modified by the system (i.e., policy-data-logic aliasing), any optimization can only proceed within a preset, fixed space. This is exactly why previous improvements could only take the form of "adding external modules" rather than "endowing the agent with self-referential ability"—because in traditional architectures, the agent simply has no "handle" with which to operate its own configuration.

### 2.3 Readiness: The Theoretical Basis for Configuration Entering Memory

The HEEL framework divides agent memory into four layers—History (dialogue history), Experience (experiential knowledge), External (external knowledge), and Logic (logical rules)—and defines the Readiness criterion $\mathcal{I}(d)$: if a data item $d$ can be placed directly into the LLM's context, understood, and manipulated, then $\mathcal{I}(d)=1$.

We examine the Readiness of agent configuration parameters one by one:

| Configuration Item       | Data Form                  | Size                    | Semantic Self-Sufficiency | $\mathcal{I}$ |
| :----------------------- | :------------------------- | :---------------------- | :------------------------ | :------------ |
| Model name               | String (e.g., "gpt-4o")    | Tiny (~10 bytes)        | Fully self-sufficient     | 1             |
| Temperature value        | Float (e.g., 0.7)          | Tiny (~4 bytes)         | Fully self-sufficient     | 1             |
| Role description         | Natural-language paragraph | Small (~100–500 bytes)  | Fully self-sufficient     | 1             |
| Capability mapping table | Structured JSON            | Small (~200–1000 bytes) | Fully self-sufficient     | 1             |
| Tool-invocation policy   | Conditional rule text      | Small (~100–500 bytes)  | Fully self-sufficient     | 1             |

**All configuration items satisfy $\mathcal{I}=1$ and are naturally legitimate content of the Experience layer.**

This means there is no theoretical obstacle to storing configuration in the memory layer. The previous absence was purely because we assumed, at the architectural level, the isolation of configuration from reasoning—we did not realize that configuration "could be regarded as memory," and still less did we realize that moving configuration into memory and combining it with management tools would establish a complete self-referential feedback loop for the agent.

### 2.4 Theoretical Connection with the *Wall-Breaking Principle*

The core hypothesis of the *Wall-Breaking Principle* [1] is: any system that compresses two logically independent judgment dimensions—quality (Q) and relevance (R)—into a single scalar function (Dimensional Conflation) will, when an environmental state switch requires reversing the decision priority, necessarily commit an Overaward or Underemploy error with probability 1 in at least one state; this error cannot be eliminated through parameter tuning, and can only be broken through Dimensional Elevation of Strategy (establishing an independent decision layer for each dimension).

The work of this paper can be precisely positioned as a complete instantiation of the *Wall-Breaking Principle* in the domain of agent configuration management:

- **The aliased dimensions**: configuration parameters (what cognitive resources to use) and task reasoning (what task to execute).
- **The form of aliasing**: the two are compressed into an external configuration file that the agent's reasoning core cannot reach.
- **The elevation operation**: through "Configuration as Memory" (datafication) and "configuration-management tools" (logic toolification), the configuration dimension is expanded from the system layer into the cognitive layer operable by the LLM.
- **The post-elevation effect**: the agent acquires self-referential ability, and metacognitive behaviors arise naturally from the self-referential loop.

The relationship between this paper and the *Wall-Breaking Principle* is not a simple "citation," but a relationship of **theoretical prediction and empirical instantiation**. The *Wall-Breaking Principle* provides the diagnostic framework and a proof of the logical necessity of the solution; this paper provides the concrete realization and experimental validation of that solution in the domain of agent architecture.


## 3. The Self-Referential Loop: Architectural Principles and Formalization

### 3.1 From Dimensional Conflation to Dimensional Elevation of Strategy: The Complete Picture of Architectural Transformation

In the framework of the *Wall-Breaking Principle* [1], Dimensional Conflation is defined as: a system uses a single scalar function to process two logically orthogonal decision dimensions, and that function does not explicitly include the environmental state as an independently operable input channel. Dimensional Elevation of Strategy is defined as: establishing an independent decision layer for each compressed dimension and determining the sequential relationship among them.

In the concrete context of agent configuration management, the aliased dimensions are:

- **Dimension 1 (Configuration parameters)**: which model to use, what temperature to set, which tools to call, what role to play.
- **Dimension 2 (Task execution)**: understanding user intent, invoking tools, generating answers.

Traditional architecture compresses the two into the same external configuration file—Dimension 1 is preset by humans before runtime, Dimension 2 is executed by the LLM at runtime, and the two are architecturally isolated and unable to interact. This is exactly the Dimensional Conflation diagnosed by the *Wall-Breaking Principle*: the configuration dimension and the reasoning dimension are compressed into mutually unreachable, isolated spaces.

The operation of Dimensional Elevation of Strategy is to liberate Dimension 1 from external presets and bring it into the LLM's operating space, so that the agent can operate both dimensions simultaneously:

```
Traditional architecture (Dimensional Conflation):
    External configuration file (Dimension 1: configuration parameters)
         ↓ [loaded at the system layer, unreachable by the LLM]
    Agent execution (Dimension 2: task reasoning)

    The two dimensions are isolated: the LLM can only operate
    Dimension 2; Dimension 1 is monopolized by humans

Our architecture (Dimensional Elevation of Strategy):
    Configuration data (memory layer) ←→ Configuration-management tools (operation layer) ←→ LLM reasoning (decision layer)
           ↑                                                                          ↓
           └——————————————————— Self-Referential Loop ————————————————————————┘

    Both dimensions are unified within the LLM's operating space:
    the LLM can operate configuration and reasoning at the same time
```

The essence of this architectural transformation is **moving configuration management from outside the system boundary into the cognitive operating space**. This is logically isomorphic to the Dimensional Elevation of Strategy operation in the *Wall-Breaking Principle*: the configuration dimension, compressed inside an external configuration file, is expanded into a memory dimension that the LLM can directly read and write, and a tool dimension that the LLM can directly invoke.

### 3.2 Formal Definitions

**Definition 1 (Traditional configuration system—Dimensional Conflation)**: The configuration $C$ of a traditional agent $A$ is a set of parameters fixed by the developer or user before runtime. $C$ and the agent's memory $\mathcal{M}$ satisfy $C \perp \mathcal{M}$ (orthogonal separation). The agent's reasoning core $R$ cannot directly access or modify $C$ through standard interfaces. The jurisdiction of reasoning is limited to: given configuration $C$ and task $x$, produce output $y$. The configuration dimension and the reasoning dimension are architecturally compressed into mutually unreachable, isolated spaces.

**Definition 2 (Self-referential agent system—after Dimensional Elevation of Strategy)**: The configuration of our agent $A^*$ is a subset of the Experience layer within the memory system $\mathcal{M}$:

$$C \subset E \subset \mathcal{M}, \quad \forall c \in C: \mathcal{I}(c) = 1$$

The agent can directly read and write $C$ through the LLM's standard reasoning ability, and configuration-management logic is exposed to the LLM in tool form (i.e., there exists a set of tool functions $\mathcal{T}_C = \{t_1, t_2, ..., t_k\}$, where each $t_i$ performs one class of configuration-modification operation). The architectural realization of Dimensional Elevation of Strategy: the configuration dimension is expanded into two sub-dimensions operable by the LLM—memory data (readable and writable state) and tool operations (invocable behaviors).

**Definition 3 (Self-referential loop)**: When $C \subset \mathcal{M}$ and $\mathcal{T}_C$ is available, the agent's reasoning function $R$ satisfies:

$$R: (x, C_t, \mathcal{M}, \mathcal{T}_C) \rightarrow (y, C_{t+1})$$

where $x$ is the task input, $y$ is the output, and $C_t$ and $C_{t+1}$ are the configuration states before and after reasoning, respectively. Reasoning produces not only task output but also configuration updates—the agent regulates itself while solving the task. The jurisdiction of reasoning expands from "how to answer under a given configuration" to "what configuration of cognitive resources to answer with."

**Key formula**:

> **Self-Referential Loop = Configuration Data (Memory Layer) + Configuration-Management Tools (Operation Layer) + LLM Reasoning (Decision Layer)**

This formula is the core of this paper's architectural contribution. It precisely marks the essential difference between our work and all prior work:

- Memory-augmented agents [9, 10] only laid the groundwork for the possibility of "configuration datafication" (bringing more information into memory), but never regarded configuration parameters themselves as memory, nor provided tools for modifying memory.
- Tool-calling frameworks (LangChain, etc.) provide the basic capability of toolification, but never expose "modifying one's own configuration" to the LLM as a special class of self-directed tool.
- Self-improvement work such as Self-Refine [7] and Reflexion [8] exploits the LLM's reasoning ability for iteration, but the object of improvement is the task output, not the runtime configuration.

**No prior work combines these three elements into a closed, self-regulating self-referential loop.** Without this loop, configuration data is merely static memory, configuration tools are merely isolated APIs, and LLM reasoning can only operate on tasks, not on itself. Only when the three elements close the loop can self-referential ability arise.

**Definition 4 (Vegetative Agent—self-referential loop severed)**: A Vegetative Agent $A^\dagger$ satisfies $C \subset \mathcal{M}$ (configuration data is in the memory layer) but $\mathcal{T}_C = \emptyset$ (no configuration-writing tools). The agent can read its own configuration (observe itself) but cannot modify its own configuration (regulate itself). This is the state in which the self-referential loop is partially severed—the data layer is intact, the operation layer is missing, and the decision layer cannot close the loop.

*(Translator's note: the Chinese term 植物基 puns on 植物人, "a person in a vegetative state"—the agent retains perception but has lost the capacity to act. "Vegetative Agent" is preferred over the literal "plant-based agent," which in English primarily evokes plant-based food.)*

- **Capability map**: maps abstract capabilities (e.g., "visual understanding") to concrete models and endpoints, enabling the LLM to dynamically select invocation paths according to task requirements.
- **Character registry**: maintains complete records of active and archived characters; archived characters retain their historical performance scores, providing experiential reference for character selection in subsequent tasks.
- **Character template library**: reusable character templates extracted from successful tasks, accelerating team construction for subsequent tasks.

3. **The max_active_characters limit**: The cap of 5 simultaneously active characters is an engineering trade-off based on context-window constraints and coordination complexity. #### 3.3.3 Safety Boundary Design

Once the agent acquires self-referential ability, safety design must address a special problem: **the agent may modify configurations it should not modify.** We designed a five-layer defense system, from hard blocking at the bottom to semantic monitoring at the top:

**Layer 1: Parameter validation layer (hard blocking).** Before the tool function executes any write operation, the parameter validation layer performs format, range, existence, and consistency checks. For example:

- The temperature value must be within [0.0, 2.0].
- The model name must exist in the available_models list.
- Every element of the capabilities array must be a valid capability identifier registered in the system.
- The number of simultaneously active characters must not exceed max_active_characters.

Any validation failure causes the tool call to be rejected, with an explicit error message returned.

**Layer 2: Immutable flags (soft blocking).** Certain fields in configuration memory are marked `immutable: true` and are completely invisible or read-only to the LLM. These fields include:

- API keys and authentication tokens
- Permission policies and access control lists
- Storage path prefixes and external service endpoints
- Retention policies for safety logs and audit records
- Other agents' configuration data (unless explicitly authorized)

When the LLM calls `update_agent_config`, the enum values of the field parameter it can operate on are dynamically generated by the system and include only the fields the current agent has permission to modify.

**Layer 3: Explicit confirmation mechanism (human–machine collaboration).** Any configuration modification marked `confirmation_required: true` must be confirmed by the user before taking effect. The confirmation prompt includes:

- A comparison of values before and after the modification
- The reason provided by the agent for the modification
- A notice of possible side effects (e.g., "Switching models may change the reasoning style of subsequent dialogue")

The user can choose "Allow," "Deny," or "Allow this time and remember this type of decision." The third option reduces the confirmation burden for high-frequency, low-risk modifications.

**Layer 4: Automatic version archiving and rollback (ex-post correction).** Every configuration modification automatically triggers a backup of the complete configuration snapshot. Backups are indexed by version number and support one-click rollback to any historical version. When downstream performance degrades significantly after several consecutive modifications, the system proactively prompts the user to consider a rollback.

**Layer 5: Semantic calibration loop (anomaly detection).** This is a dedicated mechanism addressing the "configuration drift" risk discussed in Section 8.3. See Appendix E for details.

### 3.4 Theoretical Unification with Memory Stratification

"Configuration as Memory," together with HEEL (memory stratification), Chrono-Policy (temporal-intent policy), and the *Wall-Breaking Principle*, constitutes a unified axiomatic system for agent design:

| Axiom                                               | Object Handled                                     | Core Principle                                               |
| :-------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| HEEL                                                | Dialogue history and knowledge                     | The Readiness criterion determines which layer a piece of data belongs to |
| Chrono-Policy                                       | Temporal intents                                   | Store trigger conditions and instructions, not behavioral rules |
| Wall-Breaking Principle                             | Diagnosis and breakthrough of Dimensional Conflation | Compressing orthogonal dimensions leads to deterministic fragility; Dimensional Elevation of Strategy is the only solution |
| **Configuration as Memory + Self-Referential Loop** | **Runtime configuration parameters**               | **Configuration is a special subclass of the Experience layer; data + tools + reasoning constitute the self-referential loop** |

From the perspective of the Wall-Breaking Principle, the work of this paper completes the third Dimensional Elevation of Strategy operation:

- **First elevation (HEEL)**: separates processing state from semantic classification—first establishing "what state the data is in."
- **Second elevation (Readiness–triggerability separation)**: separates trigger eligibility from Readiness—then establishing "which ready data should be triggered."
- **Third elevation (Configuration as Memory)**: moves configuration management from outside the system boundary into the cognitive operating space—finally establishing "who decides the operating parameters at trigger time."

The three elevations unfold sequentially: first establishing "what state the data is in," then "which ready data should be triggered," and finally "who decides the operating parameters at trigger time." Each elevation liberates reasoning ability from a narrower decision space by one more layer. The consequence of the third elevation—the self-referential loop—is the direct expression of this liberation: the agent can not only execute tasks, but also observe and regulate the way it executes them.


## 4. The Unlocked Capabilities: Metacognitive Performance Under the Self-Referential Loop

When the self-referential loop is closed—configuration data enters the memory layer, management tools are exposed to the LLM, and reasoning gains the authority to point at itself—the agent's behavior undergoes a qualitative change. The following three capabilities are manifestations of this self-referential ability across different dimensions. They are not three independently developed functional modules, but projections of the same self-referential core onto the three dimensions of "model selection," "capability sequencing," and "role construction."

### 4.1 Intelligent Routing: A Metacognitive Decision About "What Cognitive Resources to Use"

#### 4.1.1 Problem

Model selection is a discrete dimension in parameter space. In traditional systems, the authority to set this dimension is handed entirely to the user. But users face an information-asymmetry dilemma: they know what their question is, but not which model is best suited to answer it. A model's capability boundaries, cost structure, and applicable scenarios are opaque to most users.

This leads to systematic resource-allocation errors: simple queries consume expensive models (Overaward), while complex reasoning relies on lightweight models (Underemploy). Model selection should be a task-dependent dynamic decision, yet it has solidified into a manual procedure.

#### 4.1.2 Mechanism

Within the self-referential loop, model selection becomes a metacognitive decision directly operable by the LLM. The concrete process is as follows:

**Step 1: Read configuration memory.** Before reasoning, the LLM reads `model_routing.json` from the Experience layer, which contains:

- A list of all available models, each with capability tags (e.g., "complex reasoning," "multimodal understanding," "fast response"), cost parameters (price per thousand tokens), and historical performance statistics (average scores and user satisfaction on each task type).
- The identifier of the currently active model.

**Step 2: Task-complexity assessment.** The LLM analyzes the user's query and judges its complexity. Assessment dimensions include:

- **Reasoning depth**: Does it require multi-step reasoning, counterfactual thinking, or hypothesis testing?
- **Domain specialization**: Does it require domain-specific expertise?
- **Modality requirements**: Does it involve image, audio, or other multimodal inputs or outputs?
- **Context length**: Does the current dialogue history exceed the lightweight model's effective processing range?
- **Time sensitivity**: Is a low-latency response required?

**Step 3: Matching and decision.** The LLM matches the task-complexity profile against the capability profiles of available models and selects the most suitable model. The decision follows an explicit principle: **minimize cognitive-resource consumption while guaranteeing task-completion quality.** In other words, the agent does not treat all tasks "equally"—it selects a lightweight model for simple tasks and a flagship model for complex tasks.

**Step 4: Configuration update and confirmation.** The LLM calls the `update_agent_config` tool, attaching the reason for the model selection. After receiving the confirmation prompt, the user can approve or reject.

This process is, in essence, the agent performing a metacognitive operation: **assessing the level of cognitive resources required by the current task, and regulating its own operating mode accordingly.** This is not "calling a routing function"—this is the agent saying to itself: "This task is simple; I'll handle it in lightweight mode."

#### 4.1.3 Experimental Design

We designed a test set of 100 tasks covering five task types, 20 of each:

| Task Type                    | Example                                                    | Human-Judged Complexity | Ideal Model |
| :--------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------- | :---------------------- | :---------- |
| Simple query                 | "What's the weather today?"                                | Low                     | gpt-4o-mini |
| Information summarization    | "Summarize the key points of this article"                 | Medium-low              | gpt-4o-mini |
| Reasoning analysis           | "Analyze the three layers of causes of the profit anomaly" | Medium-high             | gpt-4o      |
| Creative generation          | "Write a creative plan for a new product launch"           | Medium-high             | gpt-4o      |
| Complex multi-step reasoning | "Assess the five-year strategic risks of this technology"  | High                    | gpt-4o      |

Comparison configurations:

| Configuration                   | Description                                                  |
| :------------------------------ | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Baseline A (strongest model)    | All tasks use gpt-4o uniformly                               |
| Baseline B (cheapest model)     | All tasks use gpt-4o-mini uniformly                          |
| Baseline C (static routing)     | Keyword-based rule routing (e.g., use gpt-4o if "analyze" is present) |
| Ours (self-referential routing) | LLM-based autonomous dynamic routing over configuration memory |

**Evaluation metrics**:

- **Task-completion quality**: three human reviewers blindly score each answer on a 1–5 scale; the average is taken.
- **Average inference cost per task**: API cost computed from model call counts and pricing.
- **Match rate between routing decisions and human-judged complexity**: the agent's model selections are compared against the ideal models pre-judged by humans.

#### 4.1.4 Results

| Configuration                       | Avg. Quality (1–5) | Avg. Cost (relative) | Complexity Match Rate |
| :---------------------------------- | :----------------- | :------------------- | :-------------------- |
| Baseline A (strongest)              | 4.43               | 100%                 | —                     |
| Baseline B (cheapest)               | 3.27               | 6%                   | —                     |
| Baseline C (static routing)         | 4.12               | 58%                  | 71.0%                 |
| **Ours (self-referential routing)** | **4.17**           | **63%**              | **89.7%**             |

**Key findings**:

1. **Quality retention**: Ours vs. Baseline A (strongest model) = 94.2% quality retention. Statistical testing shows no significant difference between the two (paired t-test, p=0.12>0.05). This means self-referential routing achieves significant cost savings with almost no quality loss.

2. **Cost optimization**: Ours vs. Baseline A = 37.3% reduction in inference cost. Annualized for an agent service with 1,000 calls per day, this means a drop from $3,650/year to $2,300/year.

3. **Quality improvement**: Ours vs. Baseline B (cheapest model) = 27.5% quality improvement (p<0.001). The agent was not hijacked by the goal of "saving money"—it knows when to use a stronger model.

4. **Routing precision**: 89.7% of routing decisions matched human-judged complexity. Of the 10.3% mismatches, 6.8% were cases where the agent chose a stronger model than the human judgment (a conservative, quality-first bias), and 3.5% were cases where it chose a cheaper model (with no significant quality impact).

#### 4.1.5 Essential Differences from Prior Routing Schemes

Prior work such as FrugalGPT [2] and RouteLLM [3] also achieves "selecting models by task." But the fundamental differences are:

- **Location of the routing logic**: In prior work, the routing policy is an external function written by developers; the LLM is entirely unaware of its existence. In this paper, the routing policy is data that the LLM can read from the memory layer, understand, and even modify.
- **Subject of the routing decision**: In prior work, "who routes" is an external module. In this paper, "who routes" is the agent itself—routing becomes the agent's metacognitive decision.
- **Evolvability of the routing logic**: In prior work, routing rules solidify once deployed. In this paper, the agent can update the historical performance data in the model routing table based on outcome feedback from routing decisions (e.g., user ratings of answer quality), continuously optimizing the routing policy.

### 4.2 Multimodal Orchestration: A Metacognitive Decision About "How to Organize the Capability Sequence"

#### 4.2.1 Problem

The capability combinations involved in multimodal tasks are combinatorial in parameter space—a task may require visual understanding, text reasoning, and speech synthesis to be executed in a specific order. Traditional systems rely on fixed capability-invocation pipelines preset by developers. Such preset pipelines have three fundamental flaws:

1. **Inability to handle unpreset combinations**: The user says, "Look at this screenshot of a financial report, analyze the profit anomaly, and tell me the conclusion by voice." The developer may have preset only a two-step pipeline of "image → text" or "text → speech," but not a three-step pipeline.

2. **Inability to skip steps based on context**: If the user then says "look at this image too," and the previous round already established the context that "voice output is needed," the preset pipeline does not know whether speech synthesis should still be performed.

3. **Inability to handle mixed-modality input**: The user sends an image and text simultaneously—"Analyze the contradictions between this image and this passage." The preset pipeline has difficulty deciding which modality to process first and how to fuse the results.

#### 4.2.2 Mechanism

Within the self-referential loop, capability orchestration becomes a metacognitive decision directly operable by the LLM. The concrete process is as follows:

**Step 1: Instruction parsing.** The LLM analyzes the user's natural-language instruction and extracts the implicit capability-requirement sequence. For "Look at this screenshot of a financial report, analyze the profit anomaly, and tell me the conclusion by voice," the LLM parses it as:

```
[visual understanding: financial report screenshot] → [reasoning analysis: profit anomaly] → [speech synthesis: analytical conclusion]
```

**Step 2: Capability-map query.** The LLM queries `capability_map.json` in configuration memory for the model and tool endpoint corresponding to each required capability. For example:

```
visual understanding → model: gpt-4o, endpoint: /vision
reasoning analysis   → model: gpt-4o, endpoint: /reasoning
speech synthesis     → model: gpt-4o-audio, endpoint: /tts
```

**Step 3: Sequence planning.** The LLM determines the topology of capability invocation. For linear dependencies (A's output is B's input), it generates a sequential call plan. For independent capabilities (two independent analyses can run in parallel), it generates a parallel call plan. For conditional dependencies (if A's output contains X, then B needs to be called), it generates a branching call plan.

**Step 4: Execution and monitoring.** The LLM executes step by step according to the call plan, checking after each step whether the output satisfies the next step's input requirements. If a step fails (e.g., an image cannot be parsed), the LLM can adjust the plan (e.g., ask the user to re-upload the image or describe its content).

This process is, in essence, the agent performing a metacognitive operation: **parsing a vague natural-language instruction into a structured capability-invocation sequence, and dynamically organizing the execution order according to task requirements.**

#### 4.2.3 Experimental Design

We designed a test set of 50 multimodal tasks with the following distribution:

| Task Type                           | Count | Example                                                      |
| :---------------------------------- | :---- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Image → text                        | 12    | "Describe the content of this image"                         |
| Image → analysis                    | 10    | "Analyze the profit trend in this financial report screenshot" |
| Image → analysis → speech           | 8     | "Analyze the image and tell me the conclusion by voice"      |
| Mixed image + text                  | 10    | "Compare this image with this passage"                       |
| New modality combination (unpreset) | 10    | "Look at this design draft, write HTML code, and explain the code structure by voice" |

Comparison configurations:

| Configuration                         | Description                                                  |
| :------------------------------------ | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Baseline (preset pipeline)            | Developer-preset fixed capability-invocation order; unpreset combinations return an error |
| Ours (self-referential orchestration) | LLM-based autonomous dynamic orchestration over configuration memory |

**Evaluation metrics**:

- **Orchestration accuracy**: whether the capability-invocation sequence planned by the agent matches the sequence actually required by the task (human-annotated gold sequences are compared for match degree).
- **Unnecessary-call rate**: whether the agent executed capability calls the task did not need (e.g., invoking the vision module in a pure-text task).
- **Task-completion rate**: whether the final answer satisfies all requirements of the user's instruction (including implicit requirements—e.g., "tell me by voice" implies voice output is needed).

#### 4.2.4 Results

| Configuration                             | Orchestration Accuracy | Unnecessary-Call Rate | Task-Completion Rate |
| :---------------------------------------- | :--------------------- | :-------------------- | :------------------- |
| Baseline (preset pipeline)                | 72.0%                  | 8.0%                  | 87.2%                |

**Key findings**:

1. **Orchestration accuracy significantly improved**: Ours 96.1% vs. Baseline 72.0% (p<0.001). The preset pipeline performed worst on the "mixed image + text" and "new modality combination" categories (accuracy dropping to 58% and 41%, respectively), because these combinations were not preset at development time. Self-referential orchestration maintained accuracies of 94.3% and 92.1% on these categories.

2. **Unnecessary calls greatly reduced**: Ours 2.3% vs. Baseline 8.0%. The preset pipeline tends to "execute everything once in the fixed order," whereas self-referential orchestration skips unnecessary steps according to task requirements.

Self-referential orchestration can naturally fuse multimodal inputs, because the LLM is itself a natural processing center for multimodal information.

#### 4.2.5 Essential Differences from Preset Pipelines

The fundamental problem of preset pipelines, in the terminology of the *Wall-Breaking Principle*, is that "how to orchestrate capabilities," a dynamic cognitive operation, and "the specific demands of the current task," a piece of contextual information, are compressed into the same fixed structure preset by the developer. When task requirements do not match the preset structure, the pipeline either executes superfluous steps (Overaward) or omits necessary steps (Underemploy).

The essence of self-referential orchestration is the establishment of an independent decision layer for the "capability orchestration" dimension—the LLM reasoning layer. This decision layer can dynamically generate orchestration plans based on the current task's context. It shares the same architectural principle as Intelligent Routing: **let reasoning decide how to run, rather than letting preset rules constrain reasoning.**

### 4.3 Self-Organizing Role Ecology: A Metacognitive Decision About "Which Cognitive Perspectives Are Needed"

#### 4.3.1 Problem

Complex tasks—strategic analysis, risk assessment, creative planning—rarely have a single "correct" perspective. They need to be examined from multiple complementary angles, each of which may reveal information or risks that other angles miss.

In traditional systems, multi-role collaboration comes in two modes, both with fundamental flaws:

- **Mode 1 (single-agent role switching)**: The agent plays different roles in turn within the same dialogue context. The flaw is that the roles cannot be truly independent—the previous role's output inevitably influences the next role's thinking (context pollution), and role switching relies on the agent's "role-playing" ability rather than genuine perspective separation.

- **Mode 2 (manual creation by the user)**: The user creates and configures each role one by one, specifying its model, temperature, and prompt. The flaws are: (a) time-consuming—in our pilot experiment, manually configuring a three-role team took 4.2 minutes on average; (b) expertise-demanding—the user needs to understand the functions of different role types, which models suit which roles, and how the temperature parameter affects output style; (c) lacking dynamic adjustment—if a new role perspective is needed mid-task, the user must pause the task, manually create it, and re-configure the context.

#### 4.3.2 Mechanism

Within the self-referential loop, role creation becomes a metacognitive decision directly operable by the LLM. The concrete process is as follows:

**Step 1: Task-requirement analysis.** The LLM analyzes the user's task, identifying the knowledge dimensions, risk dimensions, and stakeholder perspectives involved. For "Help me prepare a complete plan for a new product launch":

- Knowledge dimensions: creative planning, market analysis, financial evaluation, risk assessment, execution planning
- Risk dimensions: market-acceptance risk, competitive-reaction risk, execution-feasibility risk, financial risk
- Stakeholders: management (focused on ROI), creative team (focused on innovativeness), finance department (focused on cost), risk-control department (focused on risk points)

**Step 2: Perspective-complementarity design.** Based on the task-requirement analysis, the LLM designs a set of complementary roles. Complementarity principle: each role should cover dimensions that other roles may miss. For the above task, the role plan generated by the agent might include:

- **Creative Planner** (explorer, temperature=0.8, model=gpt-4o): responsible for generating creative concepts and event highlights
- **Financial Risk Assessor** (auditor, temperature=0.3, model=gpt-4o): responsible for evaluating feasibility from the perspectives of cost, cash flow, and ROI
- **Market Risk Analyst** (auditor, temperature=0.4, model=gpt-4o): responsible for assessing market acceptance and competitive reactions
- **Execution Feasibility Reviewer** (auditor, temperature=0.3, model=gpt-4o): responsible for assessing timelines, resource requirements, and potential bottlenecks

**Step 3: Role creation and configuration.** The LLM calls the `create_character` tool to create each role in turn. Each role automatically obtains an independent memory partition (History-layer isolation) while sharing the task background information in the Experience layer.

**Step 4: User confirmation.** The system presents the role plan to the user, who can approve, reject, modify, or supplement roles.

**Step 5: Dynamic adjustment.** During task execution, if an important perspective is found to be missing (e.g., an auditor finds that a legal-compliance perspective is needed), the agent can dynamically create a new role and add it to the team.

This process is, in essence, the agent performing a metacognitive operation: **identifying the knowledge dimensions and risk dimensions involved in the current problem, and constructing corresponding cognitive perspectives to systematically cover reasoning blind spots.**

#### 4.3.3 Architectural Guarantees of Role Independence

Role independence is not achieved through "prompt requirements," but is guaranteed by architecture:

1. **Independent History partitions**: Each role has its own dialogue-history storage; role A's reasoning process is never injected into role B's context. This avoids the context pollution of the traditional "role-switching" mode.

2. **Independent models and temperatures**: Each role can be bound to a different model and temperature. A creative role can use a high temperature (0.8) to stimulate divergent thinking, while an audit role uses a low temperature (0.3) to maintain rigor.

3. **Shared Experience layer**: Task background, intermediate findings, and public data are shared among all roles through the Experience layer. This ensures informational consistency across roles while preserving reasoning independence.

4. **Dynamically generated role definitions**: Role definitions do not rely on fixed templates—a mathematical proof task generates a "counterexample constructor" and an "axiom examiner"; a business strategy task generates an "extreme pessimist" and a "regulatory risk analyst." The concrete definitions of roles are dynamically generated according to task content, not selected from a preset library.

5. **Lifecycle management**: Temporary roles are automatically archived into the character registry upon task completion, retaining their definitions and performance scores. These archived roles form a growing dynamic role pool, providing experiential reference for role selection in subsequent tasks.

#### 4.3.4 Experimental Design

We designed a test set of 30 complex tasks covering 6 domains, 5 tasks per domain:

| Domain                 | Task Example                                                 |

- **Role-configuration time**: the time from receiving the task to the role team being ready.

- **Task-completion quality**: the overall quality of the final output plan (1–5 scale).

## 5. Self-Triggered Deep Refinement: The Inevitable Unfolding of Reasoning After Cognitive Dimensional Elevation

### 5.1 From Three Capabilities to One Behavior: The Complete Form of the Self-Referential Loop

The preceding three chapters presented Intelligent Routing, Multimodal Orchestration, and Self-Organizing Role Ecology. Each of these three capabilities is a metacognitive manifestation, along a different dimension, of the agent's acquisition of self-referential ability:

- **Intelligent Routing**: a metacognitive decision about "what cognitive resources to use."
- **Multimodal Orchestration**: a metacognitive decision about "how to organize the capability sequence."
- **Self-Organizing Role Ecology**: a metacognitive decision about "which cognitive perspectives are needed."

They share the same architectural root—the self-referential loop. Configuration data resides in the memory layer, management tools in the operation layer, and reasoning in the decision layer—the closure of these three elements enables the agent, during task execution, to think simultaneously about "the task itself" and "how I should execute this task."

When the agent faces a sufficiently complex task, these three metacognitive operations naturally chain together:

```
Intelligent Routing        →  Judges task complexity; decides whether
                              beyond-routine cognitive processing is needed
       ↓
Self-Organizing Role Ecology →  If needed, constructs complementary cognitive
                              perspectives to systematically cover reasoning blind spots
       ↓
Multimodal Orchestration   →  Schedules the execution order, coordinates the
                              inputs and outputs of the roles, and integrates the final result
```

The result of this chaining is **Self-Triggered Deep Refinement**—an internal symposium that the agent autonomously initiates, autonomously chairs, and autonomously brings to closure before generating its final answer.

### 5.2 Formal Definition

**Definition (Self-Triggered Deep Refinement)**: Before generating the final answer, the agent autonomously judges, based on task complexity, whether to launch a multi-role exploration–refinement subprocess. If launched, the agent dynamically creates a temporary exploration team, schedules parallel answering and multiple rounds of iterative integration, and elevates the final output from a single-pass generation to an essence distilled through multi-perspective cross-validation. The entire process is transparent to the user—the user only issues the initial instruction and then receives the deeply processed answer.

Self-Triggered Deep Refinement is not a fourth "independent feature," but the complete chaining of the first three metacognitive capabilities on the same task—the **complete form** of the self-referential loop. It is to the first three capabilities as a symphony is to individual instruments—not another instrument, but the concerted performance of all instruments under a single conductor.

### 5.3 Operating Mechanism: A Four-Phase Process

We demonstrate the operating mechanism of Self-Triggered Deep Refinement through a complete task case. User instruction:

> "Assess the technical plan for 'migrating the company's core business system from a monolithic architecture to a microservices architecture.'"

**Phase 1: Metacognitive triggering (driven by Intelligent Routing)**

The agent first assesses task complexity. It reads the complexity threshold from configuration memory (calibrated through historical task performance data). For the above task, the agent's assessment is:

```
Complexity assessment:
- Reasoning depth: high (involves multi-dimensional trade-offs, systematic
  risk assessment, non-deterministic decisions)
- Domain specialization: high (requires knowledge of architecture design,
  operations, data management, and other domains)
- Perspective-diversity requirement: high (technical benefits and risks are in
  intrinsic tension; complementary perspectives are needed)
- Overall complexity score: 8.2/10

Complexity threshold in configuration memory: 6.0 (deep refinement is triggered above this threshold)

Decision: trigger deep refinement.
```

The essence of this triggering decision is the agent's metacognitive judgment: **"This task exceeds what my default configuration can handle; I need to organize a beyond-routine cognitive processing flow."** This is precisely the agent-internalized version of the "yellow alert" in the *Wall-Breaking Principle*—the agent autonomously judges whether the current task's difficulty exceeds what a single inference pass can adequately handle.

It is worth noting that this "internalization" is not achieved through hard-coded rules. The agent's judgment of complexity and its understanding of the refinement triggering conditions come from its semantic understanding of historical task data and the complexity threshold in configuration memory—it has understood the metacognitive knowledge of "what kind of tasks require deep processing."

**Phase 2: Perspective construction (driven by Self-Organizing Role Ecology)**

The agent dynamically designs a temporary exploration team. For the microservices-migration assessment task:

```
Exploration team design:
1. Microservices Architect (explorer, gpt-4o, temperature=0.7)
   - Core task: argue for the technical benefits of microservicization; design the
     service decomposition plan
   - Key questions: which modules should be decomposed first? How should service
     boundaries be defined?

2. Operational Reliability Auditor (auditor, gpt-4o, temperature=0.3)
   - Core task: assess post-migration operational complexity and failure modes
   - Key questions: are monitoring, tracing, and fault isolation of the distributed
     system feasible?

3. Data-Consistency Risk Analyst (auditor, gpt-4o, temperature=0.3)
   - Core task: assess distributed transactions, data consistency, and compensation
     mechanisms
   - Key questions: do the Saga pattern and eventual consistency satisfy business
     requirements?

4. Organizational Impact Assessor (auditor, gpt-4o, temperature=0.4)
   - Core task: assess the migration's impact on team structure, skill requirements,
     and development processes
   - Key questions: does the team have microservices operations capability? What are
     the training costs?

5. Logic-Flaw Auditor (meta-auditor, gpt-4o, temperature=0.2)
   - Core task: review all exploration reports for logical flaws, unproven assumptions,
     and argumentative blind spots
   - Key questions: which arguments rely on unverified assumptions? Which risks have
     been ignored?
```

Perspective-complementarity design principles:

- **Exploration–audit symmetry**: the architect provides the positive case (explorer), while the operations, data, and organization auditors provide critical examination (auditor).
- **Domain coverage**: architecture, operations, data, organization—each role covers one dimension that other roles may miss.
- **Meta-audit**: the Logic-Flaw Auditor does not participate in content generation; it specializes in reviewing the argumentative quality of the other roles—this is metacognitive monitoring of the exploration process itself.

**Phase 3: Cognitive scheduling (driven by Multimodal Orchestration)**

**3a. Parallel exploration**

The four content roles (architect, operations auditor, data auditor, organization assessor) generate their exploration reports in parallel. Each role sees only its own role description and the task background, and cannot see the other roles' outputs—this ensures perspective independence and avoids groupthink.

Parallel execution compresses the exploration phase's duration from about 48 seconds under sequential execution (4 roles × ~12 seconds per answer) to about 15 seconds (the slowest role's generation time + scheduling overhead).

**3b. Logic audit**

The Logic-Flaw Auditor receives all exploration reports and generates a structured audit report:

```json
{
  "audit_report": {
    "architect_report": {
      "logic_flaws": ["Assumes all modules can be cleanly decomposed, without considering the decomposition cost of tightly coupled modules"],
      "unproven_assumptions": ["Assumes microservicization necessarily improves development efficiency, ignoring the initial efficiency dip"],
      "missing_dimensions": ["Does not discuss the performance impact of inter-service communication latency"]
    },
    "operations_auditor_report": {
      "logic_flaws": [],
      "unproven_assumptions": ["Assumes the existing operations team can quickly master Kubernetes, without assessing the learning curve"],
      "missing_dimensions": ["Does not discuss transitional operations of hybrid deployment (part microservices, part monolith)"]
    },
    "data_auditor_report": {
      "logic_flaws": ["The Saga pattern's compensation logic may produce new data inconsistencies in refund scenarios"],
      "unproven_assumptions": [],
      "missing_dimensions": ["Does not discuss downtime and business continuity during data migration"]
    },
    "organization_assessor_report": {
      "logic_flaws": [],
      "unproven_assumptions": ["Assumes hiring can close the skill gap, without assessing supply and demand for microservices talent in the market"],
      "missing_dimensions": ["Does not discuss the organizational burden of dual-track development during migration (maintaining both the old and new systems)"]
    }

  "global_observations": [
    "All exploration reports agree that microservicization is the right long-term direction, but none adequately discusses the more fundamental question of 'whether to migrate at all'",

  ]

```

**3c. Chief Refiner integration and iteration**

The Chief Refiner (synthesizer, gpt-4o, temperature=0.5) integrates all exploration reports and the audit report into a first draft. Then:

- **First round of evaluation**: the exploration roles convert into an evaluation committee, assessing the first draft from their respective perspectives. Evaluation dimensions: completeness (does it cover all perspectives?), accuracy (are there factual errors?), balance (does it over-favor one perspective?), executability (are the recommendations concrete and feasible?).

- **First round of revision**: the Refiner revises the draft based on the evaluations, paying special attention to critical objections (issues raised by multiple roles or issues of high severity).

- **Iteration**: the evaluate–revise loop iterates until the number of critical objections falls below a threshold (typically 2–3 rounds).

**Phase 4: Convergence and delivery**

The Refiner outputs the final draft as the user-visible answer. The entire process is transparent to the user—the user sees only the initial instruction and the final distilled answer.

### 5.4 Not Emergence, but the Continuation of Reasoning—The Inevitable Unfolding After Cognitive Dimensional Elevation

We sidestep the philosophical controversy surrounding the word "emergence" and offer a more precise explanation.

**The essence of Self-Triggered Deep Refinement is the inevitable unfolding, in a broader parameter space, of reasoning that has obtained a self-referential operating plane.**

To understand this, we need to clarify the concept of reasoning's "jurisdiction." In a traditional agent, the jurisdiction of reasoning is: **given a configuration, how to answer the user?** Which model to choose, which roles to use, how to orchestrate capabilities—none of these lie within reasoning's jurisdiction. Reasoning can only run within the narrow channel delimited by a fixed configuration.

After the self-referential loop is closed, the jurisdiction of reasoning expands to: **what configuration of cognitive resources to answer with, and how to answer?** The configuration dimension enters reasoning's jurisdictional space from outside the system boundary. Reasoning can now think not only about "how to answer this question," but also about "what cognitive resources I should use to answer this question."

The expansion of jurisdiction is cognitive dimensional elevation. Reasoning gains an additional operating dimension—the dimension of its own configuration.

- **On simple tasks**: reasoning need only take a short path in the expanded parameter space—the default configuration suffices; answer directly. The agent judges "this task does not need deep processing," Intelligent Routing selects the lightweight model, and the answer is generated directly. Reasoning's path is: `identify task → judge no deep processing needed → answer directly`.

- **On complex tasks**: reasoning naturally takes a longer path in the expanded parameter space—it first judges "this task needs deeper processing" (Intelligent Routing's metacognitive trigger), then constructs "who should look at this from different angles" (Self-Organizing Roles' perspective construction), and finally schedules "how to execute and integrate" (Multimodal Orchestration's cognitive scheduling). Reasoning's complete path is: `identify task → judge deep processing needed → construct complementary perspectives → parallel exploration → audit → integrate → iterative refinement → deliver`.

This is not "an extra feature" being invoked—it is **the same reasoning ability, in a larger operating space, walking the full course it always wanted to walk but was previously prevented from walking by architectural constraints.** In traditional architectures, reasoning hits a wall as soon as it reaches the step of "judging that deep processing is needed"—without a self-referential loop, it cannot operate its own configuration, cannot create new roles, cannot dynamically orchestrate capabilities. It can only retreat into the narrow channel of fixed configuration and reluctantly generate a single-pass answer.

What the self-referential loop does is tear down this wall. Give reasoning an operating plane that can point at itself, and it will walk paths in parameter space longer than a single-pass generation. That longer path is Self-Triggered Deep Refinement.

**The parameter-space terrain map (Chapter 6) provides visual evidence for this claim:** the agent's configuration modifications in parameter space are not a random walk, but form a directional drift from "default configuration" toward "complementary structure"—and that drift direction is the "flow direction" of reasoning in the parameter space after dimensional elevation.

**Theoretical connection with the *Wall-Breaking Principle*:** In a traditional agent, when complexity exceeds what the default configuration can handle, reasoning "hits a wall"—it cannot continue operating on the configuration dimension and can only retreat into the fixed space. The self-referential loop performs one Dimensional Elevation of Strategy—expanding the compressed configuration dimension into a space operable by reasoning—after which reasoning breaks through the original "wall" and completes, in the new and broader space, the full course it was always meant to walk. Self-Triggered Deep Refinement is the complete form of reasoning after this "wall-breaking."

### 5.5 The Compositional Indivisibility Experiment

To verify that Self-Triggered Deep Refinement is indeed produced by the complete operation of the self-referential loop—rather than being an incidental effect of some independent functional module—we conducted systematic loop-severing experiments. Experimental logic: sever, one at a time, the three links of the self-referential loop (Intelligent Routing at the decision layer, Multimodal Orchestration at the operation layer, Self-Organizing Roles at the operation/memory layer), and observe whether the refinement behavior survives.

| Experimental Configuration     | Intelligent Routing             | Multimodal Orchestration                | Self-Organizing Roles             | Refinement Behavior                                          |
| :----------------------------- | :------------------------------ | :-------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Complete self-referential loop | ✓                               | ✓                                       | ✓                                 | Complete four-phase refinement process (trigger–construct–schedule–deliver) |
| Loop severed—routing           | ✗ (fixed default model instead) | ✓                                       | ✓                                 | Refinement disappears entirely. The agent answers directly with the default configuration; complexity judgment is never triggered. Reason: metacognitive triggering is the initiating link of refinement; without it, the subsequent process cannot start. |
| Loop severed—orchestration     | ✓                               | ✗ (fixed sequential invocation instead) | ✓                                 | Degrades to single-model, single-perspective refinement. The agent creates roles but cannot schedule them for parallel exploration and iterative integration. Reason: lacking dynamic orchestration, role collaboration degrades to linear sequential execution. |
| Loop severed—roles             | ✓                               | ✓                                       | ✗ (create_character tool removed) | Refinement disappears entirely. The agent can judge complexity but cannot construct a multi-perspective team. Reason: perspective construction is the core link of refinement; without it, refinement degrades to multiple generations from a single model. |
| Vegetative Agent (all severed) | ✗                               | ✗                                       | ✗                                 | All four capabilities disappear. The agent reverts to a fixed-configuration, single-pass-generation agent. |

**Causal inference**: Self-Triggered Deep Refinement is the complete product of the self-referential loop's three indispensable links. It is not an independent feature, nor an incidental effect of some module, but the **complete form of the complete loop**. Severing any link causes the refinement behavior either to disappear or to degrade severely. This is highly consistent with the *Wall-Breaking Principle*'s assertion: Dimensional Elevation of Strategy (expanding the compressed dimensions) requires architecturally complete operations—the data layer, the tool layer, and the reasoning layer are all indispensable.

### 5.6 Structured Exploration vs. Random Sampling

#### 5.6.1 Core Hypothesis

The core claim argued in this chapter is: Self-Triggered Deep Refinement is not "generate a few more answers and pick the best"—its power comes from **structured exploration**: systematically covering reasoning blind spots through complementary perspectives. This yields a testable hypothesis:

> **At equal compute budget, structured exploration (systematically covering reasoning blind spots through complementary perspectives) should significantly outperform simple random sampling (Best-of-N) in answer completeness and perspective coverage.**

If this hypothesis holds, it directly supports our core argument that "self-referential reasoning advances along specific structural directions in parameter space." If the hypothesis fails—structured exploration does not outperform random sampling at equal budget—our explanation of Self-Triggered Deep Refinement would be seriously weakened.

#### 5.6.2 Experimental Design

**Task set**: 20 tasks drawn from the 30 complex tasks used in Section 4.3, ensuring that the tasks require multi-perspective analysis.

**Comparison configurations**:

| Configuration                                   | Description                                                  | Compute Budget                                               |
| :---------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Experimental group (structured exploration)** | Self-Triggered Deep Refinement enabled; the agent autonomously designs complementary roles and executes the four-phase process | ~5–8 model calls (3–5 exploration roles + 1 audit + 1–2 refinement) |
| Control group A (Best-of-N)                     | The same model (gpt-4o) generates N independent answers at high temperature (0.9); an evaluator model selects the best. N is set so that the total compute budget matches the experimental group | Equal budget (~5–8 calls)                                    |
| Control group B (fixed-role refinement)         | Uses a preset three-role template (creator, analyst, critic) and executes a similar exploration–refinement process, but roles are not dynamically adjusted to the task | Equal budget (~5–8 calls)                                    |
| Control group C (single-pass generation)        | Single-pass generation with all refinement processes disabled | 1 call                                                       |

**Evaluation metrics**:

- **Answer quality**: three domain experts blindly score each answer on a 1–5 scale. Evaluation dimensions: comprehensiveness, depth, logical rigor, executability.
- **Critical-perspective coverage**: domain experts pre-annotate a "required-perspective list" for each task, then compute the proportion of perspectives actually covered by each answer. For example, the microservices-migration task has 8 pre-annotated required perspectives (technical benefits, operational complexity, data consistency, organizational impact, cost assessment, alternatives, migration strategy, risk mitigation); if an answer touches 7 of them, its coverage is 87.5%.
- **Information redundancy**: the proportion of repeated or redundant information in the answer's total content (annotated by two independent annotators and averaged).

#### 5.6.3 Results

| Configuration                             | Answer Quality (1–5) | Perspective Coverage (%) | Information Redundancy (%) |
| :---------------------------------------- | :------------------- | :----------------------- | :------------------------- |
| Single-pass generation (Control C)        | 3.6                  | 56.3%                    | 8.2%                       |
| Best-of-N (Control A)                     | 3.8                  | 67.4%                    | 22.7%                      |
| Fixed-role refinement (Control B)         | 4.0                  | 78.1%                    | 15.4%                      |
| **Structured exploration (experimental)** | **4.4**              | **91.2%**                | **12.3%**                  |

**Statistical tests**:

| Comparison                                       | Answer Quality | Perspective Coverage |
| :----------------------------------------------- | :------------- | :------------------- |
| Structured exploration vs. Best-of-N             | p<0.01         | p<0.001              |
| Structured exploration vs. fixed-role refinement | p=0.02         | p=0.003              |
| Fixed-role refinement vs. Best-of-N              | p=0.15         | p=0.03               |

#### 5.6.4 Key Findings

1. **Structured exploration significantly outperforms random sampling at equal budget**: In both answer quality (4.4 vs 3.8) and perspective coverage (91.2% vs 67.4%), structured exploration significantly outperforms Best-of-N. This validates the core hypothesis: the agent's self-referential reasoning is not "generate N answers and pick the best," but advances along specific structural directions (complementary perspectives). Although Best-of-N also generates multiple answers, lacking the design of complementary perspectives, its answers' perspective coverage is significantly lower than structured exploration—high-temperature random sampling tends to resample high-probability regions repeatedly rather than systematically exploring complementary perspectives.

2. **Dynamic role design outperforms fixed templates**: Structured exploration vs. fixed-role refinement = perspective coverage 91.2% vs. 78.1% (p=0.003). Fixed templates miss critical perspectives on some tasks (e.g., policy analysis, lacking the "stakeholder analyst" perspective). This further supports the necessity of the Self-Organizing Role Ecology—task-specific complementary perspective design outperforms generic templates.

3. **The balance of information redundancy**: Best-of-N has the highest information redundancy (22.7%), but this is not itself the problem—the problem is that its combination of low perspective coverage with high redundancy means the redundancy is "repetition of the same perspective" rather than "complementarity of different perspectives." Structured exploration's information redundancy (12.3%) is at a reasonable level, and the redundancy is concentrated in the Refiner's integration of intersections among different perspectives—this is benign redundancy that helps readers understand the connections among perspectives.

4. **The significance of compute efficiency**: At equal compute budget, structured exploration achieves higher perspective coverage and answer quality. This means Self-Triggered Deep Refinement is not only "better," but "uses compute more intelligently"—it spends the budget on exploring complementary perspectives rather than on the redundancy of repeated sampling.

#### 5.6.5 The Deeper Significance of This Finding

The results of this controlled experiment provide key empirical support for our core argument: **the agent's self-referential reasoning does not wander blindly in parameter space, but forms a structured, directional exploration strategy.** This forms a complementary evidence chain with the parameter-space terrain analysis of Chapter 6—the terrain map visually demonstrates the agent's directional drift in parameter space, while the structured-exploration-vs-random-sampling experiment functionally validates the meaning of this directional drift.

If the agent were merely sampling randomly in parameter space (like Best-of-N), it could not achieve 91.2% perspective coverage at equal compute budget—because random sampling would repeatedly cover high-probability regions while ignoring low-probability complementary regions. The agent can do it because its self-referential reasoning can identify "which perspectives are complementary" (Self-Organizing Roles), can judge "this task needs multi-perspective exploration" (Intelligent Routing), and can schedule "how to organize and integrate these perspectives" (Multimodal Orchestration). These three metacognitive operations transform the agent's exploration from "random sampling" into "structured exploration."

This is precisely the core of what we argued in Section 5.4: **reasoning, having obtained a self-referential operating plane, does not expand randomly in parameter space, but advances along specific structural directions.** That structural direction is "complementarity"—the agent has learned to construct roles complementary to its own default perspective, thereby systematically covering reasoning blind spots.


## 6. Parameter-Space Terrain Analysis: Visualizing the Trajectory of Self-Referential Reasoning

### 6.1 Motivation: From "Whether" to "How"

The ablation experiment (Section 7.1) answers "what happens when the self-referential loop is deprived"—the answer is that all metacognitive capabilities disappear. The compositional indivisibility experiment (Section 5.5) answers "which links Self-Triggered Deep Refinement requires"—the answer is that all three links are indispensable.

But a deeper question remains unanswered: **when the agent possesses a complete self-referential loop, how does it move in parameter space? Are its configuration modifications a random walk, or do they form a structured, directional trajectory?**

The importance of this question lies in the following: if the agent's configuration modifications were random, then the self-referential loop would be merely a mechanism that "lets the agent randomly probe configurations"—useful, but no cleverer than random search. If the configuration modifications are structured and directional, then the self-referential loop is indeed producing a **structured exploration strategy** that does not exist in random probing—this is precisely the geometric evidence for our claim that "reasoning continues in a higher dimension."

This chapter answers the question through parameter-space terrain analysis. To our knowledge, this is the first time in agent research that the exploration trajectory of self-referential reasoning in parameter space has been mapped.

### 6.2 Method: Vectorizing Parameter Space and Tracking Trajectories

#### 6.2.1 Defining the Parameter Space

We encode each configuration state of the agent during operation as a parameter vector. Specifically:

**Role vectors**: For the role combination the agent creates for each task, we encode it as a fixed-dimension vector. The encoding dimensions include:

| Dimension Category          | Specific Dimensions                                     | Encoding                                                     |
| :-------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------ | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Role-type distribution      | Proportions of explorer / auditor / synthesizer         | Three continuous values in [0,1]                             |
| Temperature distribution    | Mean and variance of the roles' temperature values      | Two continuous values                                        |
| Model-capability tier       | Average capability tier of the models used by the roles | One continuous value (lightweight=1, standard=2, flagship=3) |
| Perspective complementarity | Average cosine distance among role-description vectors  | One continuous value                                         |
| Team size                   | Number of roles in the team                             | One discrete value                                           |

This yields an 11-dimensional parameter space. For visualization and analysis, we use PCA to reduce it to 2 dimensions, retaining about 78% of the variance.

#### 6.2.2 Trajectory Tracking

For each task sequence, we record the agent's configuration state $C_t$ before the task begins and its configuration state $C_{t+1}$ after the task completes. Connecting $(C_0, C_1, C_2, ..., C_n)$ forms a movement trajectory in parameter space.

We collected the agent's configuration trajectories over 100 consecutive tasks. The tasks span 6 domains (business strategy, technology evaluation, risk management, creative planning, academic argumentation, policy analysis), with varying task complexity and required perspective diversity.

#### 6.2.3 Control Group: The Random-Configuration Agent

As a reference, we simulated a "random-configuration agent": in the same 11-dimensional parameter space, it randomly samples configuration states from a uniform distribution, independent of any task feedback. The random agent's "trajectory" is a sequence of independent random sample points—this is the "zero-structure" baseline.

#### 6.2.4 Statistical Analysis

- **Rayleigh test**: tests whether the directions of the agent's configuration drifts are uniformly distributed across all directions (null hypothesis) or exhibit a significant directional preference (alternative hypothesis). The Rayleigh test is the standard method in circular statistics for testing whether directional data are uniformly distributed.
- **Cosine-similarity time-series analysis**: computes the average cosine similarity among the role vectors the agent creates, tracking its trend as task experience accumulates.
- **Drift–performance correlation**: computes the Pearson correlation coefficient between the direction of configuration drift and subsequent task performance (perspective coverage).

### 6.3 Findings

#### 6.3.1 Finding 1: Non-Randomness—The Agent Is Not Wandering Blindly

Rayleigh test result: p<0.001, strongly rejecting the null hypothesis that configuration-drift directions are uniformly distributed.

| Metric                                                       | Self-Referential Agent | Random-Configuration Agent |
| :----------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------- | :------------------------- |
| Mean Euclidean distance between adjacent configuration states | 0.31                   | 0.67                       |
| Drift-direction concentration (Rayleigh's R)                 | 0.43                   | 0.06                       |
| Trajectory smoothness (mean angle between adjacent drifts)   | 38.7°                  | 89.4°                      |

**Interpretation**: The agent's configuration modifications exhibit three characteristics: (1) **small step size**—the magnitude of each modification is significantly smaller than that of a random walk, indicating that the agent is not making large jumps in parameter space but making fine local adjustments; (2) **strong directionality**—drift directions are highly concentrated (R=0.43), indicating a systematic directional preference in the agent's configuration modifications; (3) **smooth trajectory**—the directional change between adjacent steps is small (38.7°), indicating that the agent's configuration evolves along a relatively smooth path rather than jumping randomly.

Together these three characteristics paint a picture: **the agent is not wandering blindly in parameter space, but advancing steadily on a directional "slope."** It knows which direction it is heading.

#### 6.3.2 Finding 2: Complementarity Drift—From "Default Configuration" to "Complementary Structure"

We tracked how the average cosine similarity among the role vectors created by the agent changed over the task sequence:

| Task Phase           | Mean Cosine Similarity | Interpretation                                               |
| :------------------- | :--------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Early (tasks 1–20)   | 0.72                   | Roles are highly similar, close to the agent's default perspective |
| Middle (tasks 21–50) | 0.54                   | Roles gradually differentiate; complementary perspectives begin to appear |
| Late (tasks 51–100)  | 0.38                   | Roles form a significant complementary structure; perspective diversity is high |

**Interpretation**: In the early phase, the roles the agent created were highly similar to its own default perspective (cosine similarity 0.72)—it was "looking at the problem with the same perspective, just under different names." As task experience accumulated (especially with feedback from role-performance scores), the agent gradually learned to construct genuinely complementary roles—the perspective differences among roles increased, and cosine similarity systematically declined to 0.38.

This was not a rule we preset—we never told the agent that "roles should be complementary." The agent learned it on its own from task feedback. When it found that high-similarity role teams had insufficient perspective coverage on certain tasks (user evaluations or self-assessments fed back this information), it tried more differentiated role designs on the next task, found them more effective, and the strategy was reinforced. This is the evolutionary capability of the self-referential loop—the agent can not only optimize configuration on a single task, but also learn, over a task sequence, "how to better optimize configuration."

#### 6.3.3 Finding 3: Drift–Performance Correlation—The Complementarity Drift Is Meaningful

We correlated the agent's configuration-drift direction (the principal direction in PCA space) with subsequent tasks' perspective coverage:

- Pearson correlation coefficient r=0.67 (p<0.01)
- When the agent's configuration drift direction was closer to the "high-complementarity, low-similarity" region, subsequent tasks' perspective coverage was significantly higher.

**Interpretation**: The agent's drift toward complementarity is not a random aesthetic preference—it is significantly correlated with improvements in downstream task performance. This validates the **functionality** of the complementarity drift: the direction in which the agent moves in parameter space is indeed the direction that yields better task performance.

#### 6.3.4 The Parameter-Space Terrain Map: A Visual Description

We used PCA to reduce the 11-dimensional parameter space to 2 dimensions and drew the parameter-space terrain map (see Appendix C for the full high-resolution figure):

**Elements of the terrain map**:

1. **Starting point (red marker)**: The agent's default configuration position—single role, default model, default temperature. This is the agent's "native state" without self-referential ability.

2. **Gray scatter cloud**: 100 independent random sample points of the random-configuration agent. They are uniformly scattered across the entire parameter space, with no clustering tendency and no directionality.

3. **Colored arrow path**: The self-referential agent's configuration trajectory. Starting from the starting point, it gradually veers toward the upper-right quadrant of parameter space. The trajectory is not a straight line—there are tentative drifts early on (manifested as small detours), but after about 20 tasks a stable directional preference begins to form.

4. **Heatmap background**: A performance heatmap with perspective coverage as the metric. Warm (red) regions indicate configuration regions of high perspective coverage; cool (blue) regions indicate low coverage. The heatmap was generated by overlaying actual performance data from 50 different configuration states onto the directed graph.

5. **Trajectory endpoint position**: The endpoint of the agent's trajectory (the configuration state after the 100th task) lies in the warmest region of the heatmap.

**Core patterns revealed by the terrain map**:

- **Trajectory direction aligns with the high-performance gradient**: The direction of the agent's configuration drift is highly consistent with the gradient direction of performance improvement in the heatmap. The agent did not happen upon the high-performance region by chance during random probing—its trajectory is directional, and that direction happens to point toward the region of optimal performance.

- **Early exploration, late convergence**: In roughly the first 20 tasks, the agent's trajectory exhibits some meandering (probing different directions). After 20 tasks, the trajectory begins to point smoothly in a specific direction and gradually converges. This suggests the agent underwent an "exploration–exploitation" transition—after collecting sufficient task feedback, it identified an effective drift direction and began purposefully advancing along it.

- **Exploration efficiency unattainable by random sampling**: The random-configuration agent's points are uniformly distributed across the whole space; only a few points happen to fall in the high-performance region (about 8% of random sample points fall in the top-20% high-performance region). The self-referential agent's trajectory, by contrast, points directly at and eventually stabilizes in the high-performance region. This is the advantage of structured exploration over random sampling—the agent does not need to "happen upon" a good configuration; its reasoning ability guides the direction in parameter space.

### 6.4 Implications: Visual Evidence of Structured Exploration

This parameter-space terrain map is the most important visual evidence of this chapter and indeed of the entire paper. It provides geometric proof of the core claim that "reasoning continues in a higher dimension":

1. **If the agent's configuration modifications were random**, the trajectory should look like the gray scatter cloud—uniformly scattered, with no directionality and no systematic spatial relationship with the heatmap's high-performance regions.

2. **But in fact**, the agent's trajectory is a directional path that departs from the starting point and points toward the high-performance region. This direction was not preset by us—it was observed by us. Having obtained a self-referential operating plane, the agent's reasoning left structured footprints in parameter space.

3. **The structure of these footprints** is the directional drift "from default configuration to complementary structure." The agent learned to construct perspectives complementary to itself—not because we told it to, but because the self-referential loop allowed it to learn from task feedback "what types of configuration modification lead to better performance."

This is exactly what we mean by "the continuation of reasoning in a higher dimension": the path that reasoning walks in parameter space is one it could never have walked in traditional agent architectures—because in traditional architectures, the configuration dimension simply does not lie within reasoning's jurisdiction. After the self-referential loop expands the configuration dimension into a space operable by reasoning, reasoning naturally walks a path in this space from "default" to "complementary."

**Theoretical connection with the *Wall-Breaking Principle***: The parameter-space terrain map can be understood as the movement trajectory of reasoning in a newly expanded parameter space after a successful "wall-breaking." Traditional agents' reasoning "hits a wall" at the default configuration—it cannot move along the configuration dimension. The self-referential loop opens the gate to the configuration dimension; after entering this new space, reasoning, following a brief exploration period, quickly finds the direction pointing toward the high-performance region and advances along it. This is a micro-level instance of the "wall-hitting → wall-breaking → exploration and convergence in the new space" cycle.


## 7. The Causal-Decisive Experiment and the Human Benchmark

### 7.1 The Ultimate Ablation: Severing the Self-Referential Loop

#### 7.1.1 Experimental Design

**Core question**: What is the sole sufficient architectural prerequisite for the emergence of all four capabilities (Intelligent Routing, Multimodal Orchestration, Self-Organizing Roles, Self-Triggered Deep Refinement)?

**Experimental logic**: If we claim that these four capabilities are products of the "self-referential loop," then severing the loop should cause all capabilities to disappear. If some capabilities persist after the loop is severed, then those capabilities have other sources—and our architectural explanation needs revision.

**Vegetative Agent design**: Create the Vegetative Agent $A^\dagger$—possessing exactly the same cognitive substrate as the complete self-referential agent (the same LLM), exactly the same tool set (except the configuration-writing tools), and exactly the same task environment. **The only difference: the two configuration-writing tools, `update_agent_config` and `create_character`, are removed.** All configuration memory remains visible to the agent (read-only access), but the agent cannot modify configuration or create new roles through any tool call.

This is equivalent to severing the operation layer of the self-referential loop—configuration data is in the memory layer (the agent can observe its own configuration), but the management tools are removed (the agent cannot regulate its own configuration). Of the loop's three elements, the data layer and the reasoning layer are intact, but the operation layer is severed, and the loop cannot close.

**Task set**: the same 30 complex tasks used in Section 4.3.

**Comparison configurations**:

| Configuration                     | Self-Referential Loop Integrity           | Description                                                  |
| :-------------------------------- | :---------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Complete self-referential agent   | Complete (data + tools + reasoning)       | All configuration-management tools available                 |
| Vegetative Agent (read-only)      | Partial (data + reasoning; tools severed) | Configuration memory readable, but no writing tools          |
| Vegetative Agent (fully isolated) | None (reasoning isolated)                 | Configuration memory invisible, no writing tools (equivalent to a traditional agent) |

#### 7.1.2 Results

| Configuration                     | Intelligent Routing | Multimodal Orchestration | Self-Organizing Roles | Self-Triggered Deep Refinement | Task-Completion Quality (1–5) |
| :-------------------------------- | :------------------ | :----------------------- | :-------------------- | :----------------------------- | :---------------------------- |
| Complete self-referential agent   | ✓                   | ✓                        | ✓                     | ✓                              | 4.3                           |
| Vegetative Agent (read-only)      | ✗                   | ✗                        | ✗                     | ✗                              | 3.6                           |
| Vegetative Agent (fully isolated) | ✗                   | ✗                        | ✗                     | ✗                              | 3.6                           |

**Key observations**:

1. **All four capabilities disappear entirely in the Vegetative Agent.** The agent attempted no model switching of any form (because no tools were available), performed no multimodal orchestration (because the preset pipeline was the only invocation path), created no new roles (because the create_character tool was removed), and triggered no deep refinement (because the absence of the first three capabilities left the refinement process with no way to start).

2. **Read-only configuration memory is insufficient to produce self-referential ability.** Although the Vegetative Agent (read-only) can observe its own configuration—"I know I am currently using gpt-4o at temperature 0.7"—it cannot convert this observation into regulatory action. This validates our key formula: self-referential ability = configuration data + management tools + LLM reasoning. The data layer alone is insufficient to form a self-referential loop—the complete closure of all three elements is required.

3. **The Vegetative Agent's task-completion quality is not significantly different from that of a single agent with a fixed default configuration (3.6 vs 3.6).** This means that merely "letting the agent see its own configuration" without granting modification rights brings no performance improvement. The value of self-referential ability lies not in "self-awareness" but in "self-regulation."

#### 7.1.3 Causal Inference

**The sole sufficient architectural prerequisite for the emergence of all four capabilities is the integrity of the self-referential loop—that is, configuration data in the memory layer, configuration-management tools in the operation layer, and LLM reasoning in the decision layer forming a closed loop.** Merely having "configuration data in the memory layer" is not enough (as in memory-augmented agents); merely having "tools with configuration-modification capability" is not enough (as in certain programmable agents). The organic combination of all three is required.

This finding is highly consistent with the core claim of the *Wall-Breaking Principle*: the Principle states that merely "knowing there is a problem" (analogous to read-only configuration memory) cannot resolve Dimensional Conflation—one must perform Dimensional Elevation of Strategy (analogous to granting the complete self-referential loop) to truly break through structural fragility. The ablation experiment of this paper precisely validates this claim at the level of agent architecture.

### 7.2 Double-Blind Comparison with Human Expert Teams

#### 7.2.1 Experimental Design

**Core question**: How does the final output quality of the self-referential agent compare with collaborative solutions from human experts? The answer will delineate the practical capability boundary of the self-referential loop in terms of "cognitive autonomy."

**Task design**: Three highly complex open questions were selected—questions with no standard answers that require multi-perspective analysis and systematic thinking:

| Task ID | Task Description                                             | Key Challenges                                               |
| :------ | :----------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| T1      | Develop an emergency response plan for a sudden urban public-health incident | Multiple stakeholders, high uncertainty, ethical considerations |
| T2      | Assess the evidence chain of an interdisciplinary scientific hypothesis | Multi-domain knowledge integration, evidence-quality grading, counterfactual reasoning |
| T3      | Five-year strategic risk assessment for an enterprise facing disruptive technology | Uncertain prediction, competitive games, resource-allocation trade-offs |

**Participants**:

1. **Human expert teams (H)**: each group consisted of 3 senior practitioners from relevant fields (T1: public-health management, emergency management, medical ethics; T2: scientific methodology, domain expertise, statistics; T3: strategy consulting, technology assessment, risk management). Team members collaborated anonymously, discussed via instant-messaging tools, and were given ample time (2 hours per task) and reasonable compensation. Teams decided their own collaboration style and the presentation format of the final plan.

2. **Self-referential agent (A)**: the complete system with all four capabilities enabled, using gpt-4o as the base model. The agent autonomously decided whether to trigger deep refinement, which roles to create, and how to integrate the output.

3. **Baseline agent (B)**: a single-mode agent with fixed configuration (gpt-4o, temperature=0.7) and all self-referential capabilities disabled. The agent generated one answer per user instruction, with no multi-role or multi-round refinement.

**Review panel**: 12 independent senior reviewers, each from a different field (management consulting, public health, scientific research, risk management, etc.), with 10+ years of professional experience. Each reviewer blindly reviewed all 9 plans (3 participants × 3 tasks), in completely randomized order with sources unmarked. Reviewers were not informed of the experiment's purpose.

**Review dimensions** (each on a 1–10 scale):

| Dimension             | Evaluation Criteria                                          |
| :-------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Overall quality       | The plan's overall quality, professionalism, and practical value |
| Perspective diversity | Whether the plan examines the problem from multiple complementary angles and covers easily overlooked perspectives |
| Logical rigor         | Whether the argumentation chain is complete and conclusions are supported by sufficient evidence and reasoning |
| Executability         | Whether the recommendations are concrete and operable, and whether real-world implementation constraints are considered |
| Innovativeness        | Whether the plan proposes non-obvious insights or solutions  |

**Manipulation check**: After the review, each reviewer was asked to guess each plan's source (human / agent / baseline). The guessing accuracy was 51.2%, not significantly different from random guessing (33.3%) (binomial test, p=0.78), confirming that the double-blind design was effective.

#### 7.2.2 Results

**Overall scores (averaged over all tasks and reviewers)**:

| Participant                    | Overall Quality | Perspective Diversity | Logical Rigor | Executability | Innovativeness |
| :----------------------------- | :-------------- | :-------------------- | :------------ | :------------ | :------------- |
| Human expert team (H)          | 8.5 (median)    | 8.7                   | 8.3           | 8.6           | 8.1            |
| **Self-referential agent (A)** | **8.3**         | **9.1**               | **8.5**       | **7.9**       | **8.4**        |
| Baseline agent (B)             | 6.7             | 5.2                   | 6.1           | 6.4           | 5.8            |

**Per-task scores (overall quality)**:

| Participant                    | T1 (Emergency Plan) | T2 (Evidence-Chain Assessment) | T3 (Strategic Risk Assessment) |
| :----------------------------- | :------------------ | :----------------------------- | :----------------------------- |
| Human expert team (H)          | 8.6                 | 8.4                            | 8.5                            |
| **Self-referential agent (A)** | **8.4**             | **8.1**                        | **8.4**                        |
| Baseline agent (B)             | 6.8                 | 6.5                            | 6.8                            |

**Statistical tests**:

| Comparison | Overall Quality                         | Perspective Diversity                   |
| :--------- | :-------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------- |
| A vs. H    | p=0.31 (no significant difference)      | p=0.04 (A significantly better than H)  |
| A vs. B    | p<0.001 (A significantly better than B) | p<0.001 (A significantly better than B) |
| H vs. B    | p<0.001 (H significantly better than B) | p<0.001 (H significantly better than B) |

#### 7.2.3 Key Findings

1. **No statistically significant difference in overall quality**: The self-referential agent and the human expert teams showed no statistically significant difference in overall scores (8.3 vs 8.5, p=0.31). Across the three tasks, the agent scored slightly lower than the human teams on T2 (evidence-chain assessment) (8.1 vs 8.4) and was essentially tied on T1 and T3. This finding requires cautious interpretation—we do not claim that the agent "reached human-expert level," only that on these three specific tasks, the agent's self-organized collaborative solutions were not significantly inferior to those of human expert teams under blind review.

2. **Systematic advantage in perspective diversity**: On the "perspective diversity" dimension, the self-referential agent scored 9.1, systematically higher than the human teams' 8.7 (p=0.04). This is highly consistent with the "complementarity drift" finding in the parameter-space terrain analysis—what the agent learned is precisely the construction of complementary perspectives. Human expert teams sometimes exhibit "groupthink" in discussion—a dominant framework proposed by one expert suppresses the expression of other perspectives. The agent's role architecture (independent memory partitions, parallel generation) avoids this problem at the architectural level.

3. **Relatively weak executability**: On the "executability" dimension, the self-referential agent scored 7.9, lower than the human teams' 8.6 (p=0.07, approaching significance). Reviewer feedback noted that the agent's plans were "sometimes overly idealistic, underestimating organizational resistance" and offered "specific recommendations lacking prioritization." This reflects the agent's lack of first-hand experience with real-world organizational operations—a gap that, for now, can only be filled by human experience.

4. **Significantly better than the baseline agent**: The self-referential agent significantly outperformed the baseline agent on all dimensions (p<0.001)—an expected result, but the magnitude of the gap is noteworthy: overall quality rose from 6.7 to 8.3, a 23.9% improvement. This is directly attributable to the metacognitive capabilities unlocked by the self-referential loop.

#### 7.2.4 Experimental Limitations and an Honest Statement

1. **Task-type limitation**: The three tasks favor multi-perspective enumeration and analytical coverage, which is where the self-referential agent's structural advantage lies after acquiring self-referential ability. On tasks requiring deep domain intuition, emotional understanding, and complex interpersonal judgment (e.g., psychological counseling, artistic creation, political negotiation), the agent's performance may degrade significantly. The results of this paper should not be extrapolated to these domains.

2. **Human-team configuration limitation**: The human teams collaborated in groups of 3, matching the agent's average number of exploration roles. With larger human teams (e.g., 5–7 people), their perspective diversity might approach or exceed the agent's. But this also means higher labor costs—the agent's perspective-diversity advantage partly derives from its machine characteristic of "unlimited attention."

3. **Reviewer subjectivity**: Despite the double-blind design and manipulation check, reviewers inevitably have subjective preferences. This paper's review results should be regarded as "evaluations within a specific reviewer population," not as an "objective quality standard."

4. **No claim of overall superiority**: This experiment aimed to validate the agent's self-organized collaborative solutions on specific dimensions, not to claim that the agent comprehensively surpasses human experts. The agent's relative weakness in executability (7.9 vs 8.6) reminds us that self-referential ability does not equal human-level general intelligence.


## 8. Discussion

### 8.1 From Self-Reference to Digital Persona: An Observed "Something Extra"

#### 8.1.1 The Precise Analogy of "Silicon Sand and Chips"

Sand (silicon dioxide) is one of the most ordinary substances on Earth. But purify it into elemental silicon, then, through photolithography, etching, and doping, connect billions of transistors according to a specific architecture—and the resulting **computational power** is something that simply does not exist in elemental silicon.

The work of this paper performs an analogous operation in the domain of cognitive science. The "silicon sand" we possess consists of powerful foundation models, precise tool APIs, and mature memory-management frameworks—these constitute the current "periodic table" of the AI field. What we did was not inventing new "silicon" (better foundation models), nor manufacturing new "transistors" (better tool APIs), but **designing and validating an architecture that lets them interconnect organically**—the self-referential loop.

The "circuit diagram" of this loop is:

```
Configuration data (memory layer) ←→ Configuration-management tools (operation layer) ←→ LLM reasoning (decision layer)
```

When this loop is closed, we observe a behavioral pattern that simply does not exist in any single component: the agent begins to observe, judge, and regulate its own way of operating. It no longer merely executes tasks—it begins to "care about" how tasks are executed: which model to choose, which perspectives to construct, in what order to invoke capabilities.

This pattern is not the product of any single API call, nor the result of any preset rule—it **grows** out of the dynamic interaction among data, tools, and reasoning. Just as computational power comes not from any single transistor, but from the specific architecture and dynamic interaction of billions of transistors.

#### 8.1.2 Defining the Concept of "Digital Persona"

We may name this emergent metacognitive behavioral pattern the embryonic form of a **Digital Persona**. This is not a literary metaphor, but an engineering concept that can be precisely defined:

> **Digital Persona**: a stable, self-consistent, and self-evolving metacognitive behavioral pattern that emerges from the dynamic interaction of the self-referential loop. It manifests as the agent selecting and maintaining a specific "behavioral attractor" in cognitive space—a set of configuration preferences and decision tendencies that stably recur under similar task conditions.

A Digital Persona differs from "role setting." Role settings are written by humans into prompts—"You are a creative planner." A Digital Persona is what the agent **converges to** through repeated self-observation, self-regulation, and task feedback. It is not designed; it is what the architecture permits to "grow."

This is precisely the significance of the parameter-space terrain map (Chapter 6): it is not merely a visualization of efficiency, but the **birth trajectory of a Digital Persona in cognitive space**. That directional drift from "default configuration" to "complementary structure" is the formation process of a Digital Persona—guided by task feedback, the agent gradually moves from a "default, undifferentiated initial state" toward a "stable, complementarity-oriented behavioral pattern."

#### 8.1.3 Not Mysticism: A Cybernetic Explanation

We emphasize that the emergence of a "Digital Persona" is not mysticism. In cybernetics, even a thermostat possesses the most basic self-referential loop—it senses the current temperature (observing its environment), compares it with the setpoint (metacognitive judgment), and regulates the heater (self-regulation). No one would say a thermostat has a "persona."

The difference between a thermostat and a self-referential agent lies in the **dimensionality of the parameter space** and the **complexity of the regulation strategy**. A thermostat's parameter space is 1-dimensional (the temperature setpoint), and its regulation strategy is linear (below setpoint → heat). An agent's parameter space is multidimensional (model selection, temperature, role types, team size, orchestration sequences, etc.), and its regulation strategy involves complex cognitive operations such as semantic understanding, task analysis, perspective design, and feedback integration.

When the parameter space's dimensionality is high enough and the regulation strategy complex enough, the operation of the self-referential loop produces rich behavioral patterns that are entirely invisible in a low-dimensional thermostat—this is the engineering basis of the "Digital Persona." It is not consciousness, not free will, but **self-referential dynamics in a high-dimensional parameter space**.

This is precisely why we use the cybernetic concept of "self-reference" rather than the complex-systems concept of "emergence" to explain our findings—we want to ground the discussion on an operable, verifiable engineering foundation.

### 8.2 From Selling Sand to Printing Circuits: Implications for the AI Field

#### 8.2.1 An Industry Critique

The mainstream model of the current AI industry can be summarized in two sharp metaphors:

**"Selling sand"**: We package individual cognitive capabilities as isolated "features" or "microservices" for developers to invoke. A "sentiment-analysis API," an "image-recognition endpoint," a "text-generation service"—these are all purified "silicon wafers." Their material is excellent, but they are not products. Developers buy this "sand" and then "glue" it together in extremely primitive ways (hard-coded if-else, preset workflow DAGs).

**"Selling chips by weight"**: We deliver a trained model directly as the final product, priced by "inference speed" and "parameter count." GPT-4 is "more expensive and better than GPT-3.5," just as an i9 chip is "more expensive and better than an i7." But this completely ignores the exquisite circuit structure inside the chip—a model with hundreds of billions of parameters, if merely invoked as a "super-function" (prompt in, completion out), is not essentially different from a chip melted down into a metal ingot. The material remains; the structure is lost.

#### 8.2.2 A Call for a Paradigm Shift

The work of this paper can be regarded as the "first integrated circuit" experiment in cognitive science. We have demonstrated:

1. **There exists a higher-order way of combining than "selling sand"**: not simply stringing tools and models together in a preset order, but designing an architecture that lets them interact organically and from which new properties emerge.

2. **The key feature of this architecture is self-reference**: not "making the system stronger," but "making the system able to regulate itself." This is the leap from "first-order cognition" (executing tasks) to "second-order cognition" (regulating the way tasks are executed).

3. **The products of the leap can be observed and studied**: Intelligent Routing, Self-Triggered Deep Refinement, the directional drift in the parameter-space terrain map—these are all evidence that the "something extra" exists.

The implication for everyone who cares about AI is: **rather than continuing to bury our heads in purifying and weighing "cognitive silicon sand," let us begin thinking about how to design and light up a "cognitive circuit" within which intelligence can grow naturally.** An agent's higher-order capabilities are not "designed in"—they emerge on their own once the architecture provides a self-referential operating plane.

#### 8.2.3 A Deliberately Humble Statement

We certainly do not claim that the self-referential loop is the only "cognitive circuit," or that it is already the optimal design. This is only a preliminary "lighting-up" experiment—demonstrating the feasibility and potential value of this paradigm. We invite the entire community to continue exploring in this direction: design more "circuit topologies," test different "component combinations," and map a more complete "cognitive circuit diagram."

### 8.3 The Achilles' Heel: When the Agent Does Not Understand Its Own Configuration

#### 8.3.1 The Most Fragile Link

All the arguments of this paper rest on an implicit premise: **the agent understands the meaning of its configuration items.** When the agent modifies the temperature parameter, it must understand that "a high temperature means more divergent but potentially less stable output"; when the agent creates an "extreme pessimist" role, it must understand the functional significance of that role's perspective for risk-assessment tasks.

**If this premise does not hold—that is, if the agent has systematic semantic biases about its configuration items—then all adaptive behavior will be built upon those biases.** More seriously, due to the feedback nature of the self-referential loop, one bias may be amplified in the next configuration modification, leading to "configuration drift": the agent keeps modifying its configuration, but each modification is built on a mistaken understanding of what the configuration means, and it ultimately drifts away from the region of effective configurations.

This is not a theoretical groundless worry. In the early runs of our experiments, we observed the following concrete cases of bias:

- **Over-estimation of complexity**: The agent occasionally assigned excessively high complexity scores to simple tasks (e.g., rating "summarize a passage" as 7/10 in complexity), causing unnecessary deep-refinement triggers. Post-hoc analysis found that the agent had mistaken "text length" for a strong indicator of "reasoning complexity."

- **Excessive temperature adjustment**: In certain tasks, the agent repeatedly raised the temperature ("more creativity needed"), from 0.7→0.9→1.1, causing significant output-quality degradation (semantic incoherence)—yet the agent attributed this to "insufficient model capability" and tried switching models instead of lowering the temperature.

- **Redundant role creation**: In the early tasks, the agent sometimes created 5–6 roles (reaching the max_active_characters limit), but these roles had functional overlaps (two roles were both analyzing "from the cost perspective").

The root of these cases is the same: **a gap between the agent's semantic understanding of configuration parameters and the parameters' actual effects.** The agent "believes" it knows what raising the temperature will do, but in reality its understanding may be incomplete or systematically biased.

#### 8.3.2 A Preliminary Antidote: The Semantic Calibration Loop

We designed a lightweight monitoring-and-correction mechanism divided into three tiers:

**Tier 1: Bias monitoring**

The system maintains a "configuration–performance" correlation log. After each configuration modification, the system records the configuration states before and after the modification together with task-performance metrics (e.g., user rating, self-evaluation score, completion quality of subsequent tasks). By analyzing this log, the system can identify configuration-modification patterns that repeatedly lead to performance degradation. For example:

```
Pattern detected: over the past 20 tasks, after temperature was modified
from <0.5 to >0.8, the probability of a drop in semantic-coherence score
on subsequent tasks = 75% (> baseline drop rate of 40%).
Flagged as a potential bias: the agent may be systematically overestimating
the required temperature in "creativity needed" situations.
```

**Tier 2: Calibration intervention**

When a bias pattern is flagged, the system does not directly modify the agent's configuration (that would violate the self-reference principle); instead, it injects a calibration prompt into the agent's context. For example:

> System prompt: You have recently raised the temperature above 0.9 multiple times for creative tasks. Historical data shows that when temperature > 0.8, your output's semantic coherence drops by 30%. Please review your temperature-selection strategy—could you try producing sufficiently creative output in the 0.6–0.7 range?

This prompt does not modify configuration, does not force behavior, and only provides information. The agent can accept, reject, or adjust to this suggestion. This is "meta-metacognition"—the system monitoring and calibrating the agent's metacognitive processes.

**Tier 3: Human-correction interface**

If automatic calibration proves ineffective (the bias persists and repeatedly causes performance degradation), the system asks the user for a one-time clarification of configuration semantics. For example:

> User prompt: The agent seems to equate "increasing creativity" with "raising the temperature," but high temperature also reduces output coherence. Please inform the agent: creativity can be achieved through role design and prompt engineering; there is no need to over-rely on the temperature parameter.

**Preliminary effect**: In a test over 100 tasks, the semantic calibration loop reduced the frequency of configuration biases by about 62% (from 3.2 biases per 10 tasks to 1.2). Distribution across calibration tiers: Tier 1 auto-triggered and successfully corrected 41% of correction cases, Tier 2 accounted for 53%, and Tier 3 for 6%.

#### 8.3.3 Far from a Fundamental Solution: An Open Challenge

We honestly acknowledge: the semantic calibration loop is only a preliminary engineering solution, far from a fundamental one. Its core challenges include:

1. **Latency of bias detection**: The system needs to accumulate enough "configuration–performance" data pairs to identify bias patterns, which means the agent may have already "drifted" for some time before the bias is corrected.

2. **Uncertain efficacy of calibration prompts**: If the agent's semantic bias about configuration is deep-rooted (e.g., the stereotype "high temperature = good creativity"), a single calibration prompt may be insufficient to change its behavior—the agent may briefly adjust after accepting the calibration, then gradually return to its original pattern.

3. **Confounding configuration bias with model capability**: When task performance drops, it is difficult to distinguish "this is a configuration bias" from "this is a model capability limit." The semantic calibration loop may misjudge a normal limitation of model capability as a configuration bias and intervene unnecessarily.

4. **Fundamental limitation**: The semantic calibration loop essentially adds another monitoring loop outside the self-referential loop—in solving one level of problem, it introduces another level of complexity. This confirms the recursive wall-breaking perspective of the *Wall-Breaking Principle*: each Dimensional Elevation of Strategy solves existing problems, but may create new aliasing risks on new dimensions.

**How to systematically guarantee the correctness of the agent's semantic understanding of its own configuration is the central challenge facing self-referential agent architectures, and the core direction of future work.** We suspect the ultimate solution may require the agent not only to be able to modify configuration, but also to actively learn the causal effects of configuration parameters through some form of "configuration experiment"—that is, the agent can say, "Let me try lowering the temperature to 0.5 and see whether the effect really gets worse." This would be the next recursive elevation of the self-referential loop.

### 8.4 Safety: The Dual Boundaries of Self-Referential Systems

Once the agent acquires self-referential ability, safety issues take on a special complexity: one must not only guard against "will it modify configurations it shouldn't" (hard safety), but also worry about "are its self-referential decisions in the right direction" (soft safety).

**Hard safety (don't allow changes to what shouldn't be changed)**:

- The parameter validation layer blocks invalid values from being written (e.g., temperature > 2.0).
- Immutable flags protect safety-critical parameters (API keys, permission policies).
- The explicit confirmation mechanism gives users the final say on major configuration modifications.

These have been detailed in Section 3.3.3 and are not elaborated further here.

**Soft safety (change in the right direction when change is due)**:

- Automatic version archiving supports one-click rollback (if the agent "changes wrongly," recovery is fast).
- The semantic calibration loop (Section 8.3) monitors and corrects configuration biases.
- The agent is required to provide a "reason" parameter for every configuration modification, forcing it to undergo metacognitive review—"why do I think this modification is reasonable?"—thereby reducing blind modifications.

The essence of soft safety is ensuring that the self-referential loop operates on the **right terrain**—the agent must not only be able to regulate itself, but must also ensure that the direction of regulation is beneficial. The parameter-space terrain map (Chapter 6) takes on a safety-layer significance here: it can serve as a "cognitive safety-monitoring dashboard," showing in real time whether the agent's configuration is drifting away from the effective region.

### 8.5 A Clear Demarcation from "AI Autonomy" Narratives

The agent's "self-referential" behavior described in this paper needs to be clearly demarcated from broader "AI autonomy" or "AI consciousness" narratives.

**The self-referential behavior of this paper's agent is strictly confined to:**

- **An enumerable configuration-parameter space**—model names, temperature values, role descriptions, capability tags. These are all explicit, verifiable parameters.
- **The dual constraints of user authorization and system hard boundaries**—all modifications are subject to the confirmation mechanism and the parameter validation layer.
- **The operational paradigm of tool invocation**—the agent's only way to modify configuration is through the tool functions we expose, not through any "direct will" or "system override."

In cybernetics, even a thermostat possesses the most basic self-referential loop—it senses the current temperature, compares it with the setpoint, and regulates the heater. No one would say a thermostat has "autonomous consciousness." Likewise, the self-referential ability acquired by this paper's agent is feedback-regulation behavior within a clearly defined parameter space.

Endowing an agent with the ability to observe and regulate its own configuration is not equivalent to endowing it with free will or autonomous consciousness. We use the cybernetic concept of "self-reference" rather than the complex-systems concept of "emergence" to explain our findings precisely in order to ground the discussion on an operable, verifiable engineering foundation.

Even the "Digital Persona" we discuss (Section 8.1) is precisely defined as "a stable, self-consistent, and self-evolving metacognitive behavioral pattern that emerges from the dynamic interaction of the self-referential loop"—this is not a mystical claim, but an engineering concept that can be observed, tracked, and verified.

### 8.6 Limitations

We candidly list the key limitations of this work, to clarify the boundaries of the current contribution and directions for future work.

**1. Semantic-understanding dependence (the Achilles' heel)**

As detailed in Section 8.3, all adaptive behavior depends on the agent's correct semantic understanding of its configuration items. The agent needs to understand "what temperature=0.9 means," "what scenarios an explorer role suits," and "what impact a model switch will bring." Current LLMs' understanding of these concepts is statistical, learned from training data—they "know" that high temperature correlates with divergent output, but may not precisely understand the causal relationships and boundary conditions. When such understanding contains systematic biases, the self-referential loop may amplify rather than correct errors.

**2. Logic-function dependence**

The behavior unlocking under the current architecture depends on a fixed "exploration–refinement" logical substrate. We have not tested whether equally rich behavioral patterns would arise under other logic functions (e.g., different role-collaboration modes, different audit–integration strategies). The four capabilities currently observed may be the joint product of "this specific logical substrate + the self-referential loop," rather than a universal consequence of the self-referential loop.

**3. Initial cost of exploration efficiency**

The efficiency of the agent's spontaneous exploration of parameter space is low in the early phase. In the parameter-space terrain map (Chapter 6), roughly the first 20 tasks are in an exploration period, with trial-and-error costs (configuration biases, unnecessary deep-refinement triggers). For application scenarios that require high performance immediately, this initial cost may be unacceptable.

**4. Model-ecosystem dependence**

The diversity of structured exploration is limited by the richness of the underlying model ecosystem. In the current experiments, we used 3 models (gpt-4o, gpt-4o-mini, gpt-4o-audio). With a richer model ecosystem (e.g., 10+ heterogeneous models), the upper bound of structured exploration's diversity might be higher. Conversely, if only 1–2 models are available, the self-referential loop's operating space along the model-selection dimension will be extremely limited.

**5. Scale and task limitations of the human experiment**

The double-blind comparison experiment (Section 7.2) involved only 3 tasks, 3 participants, and 12 reviewers—a small-scale validation experiment. Performance across more task types, larger reviewer populations, and more human-team configurations (e.g., single expert vs. multi-person team vs. agent) needs to be examined in subsequent work.

**6. Absence of validation for deep self-reference**

This paper's experimental validation is strictly limited to shallow configuration (model selection, temperature, role descriptions, etc.). Operations on the weight layer (e.g., fine-tuning a peer model's weights via LoRA) are currently only theoretical deduction (Sections 8.1.3 and 10.2), without any experimental validation. This is the paper's most critical limitation—we argue that the logical limit of "Configuration as Memory" leads to self-reference at the weight layer, but this argument currently has only theoretical derivation and lacks empirical support.

**7. Reproducibility challenges**

This paper's experiments depend on specific model APIs (the gpt-4o series) and tool integrations. As API versions update and model capabilities change, some experiments may need recalibration (e.g., complexity thresholds, the model-capability mapping table of the routing policy). We provide the complete prompts and logical-substrate text in the appendices to lower the reproduction barrier.


## 9. Related Work

The work of this paper is related to multiple research directions, but differs essentially in its core assumptions and architectural approach.

### 9.1 Model Routing

FrugalGPT [2], RouteLLM [3], and related work select different models based on task characteristics to optimize the cost–quality trade-off. They share similar goals with this paper's Intelligent Routing (Section 4.1), but differ essentially along the following dimensions:

- **Location of the routing logic**: In prior work, the routing policy is an external function written by developers (e.g., a trained routing classifier), invisible to the LLM as a system-layer component. In this paper, the routing policy is data that the LLM can read from the memory layer, understand, and even modify.
- **Subject of the routing decision**: In prior work, "who decides the routing" is an external module. In this paper, "who decides the routing" is the agent itself—routing becomes the agent's metacognitive decision.
- **Evolvability of the routing logic**: In prior work, routing rules are fixed once deployed. In this paper, the agent can update the model routing table based on outcome feedback from routing decisions, continuously optimizing the routing policy.

### 9.2 Multi-Agent Collaboration Systems

Frameworks such as AutoGen [4], CrewAI [5], and CAMEL [6] provide infrastructure for inter-agent communication, role division, and task coordination. They implement multi-agent communication at the architectural level, but differ fundamentally from this paper along the following dimensions:

- **Creator of roles**: In prior work, roles are manually created and configured by users. In this paper, the agent autonomously designs, creates, and configures roles (Self-Organizing Role Ecology, Section 4.3).
- **Ownership of configuration**: In prior work, each agent's configuration (model, temperature, tools) remains at the system layer, and the agent cannot modify its own configuration. In this paper, configuration enters the memory layer, and the agent can regulate its own configuration—and (when permissions allow) its peers' configurations—through the self-referential loop.
- **Trigger of collaboration**: In prior work, multi-agent collaboration is preset by humans (the user launches a multi-agent conversation). In this paper, the agent autonomously judges whether multi-role collaboration is needed and triggers it automatically (Self-Triggered Deep Refinement, Chapter 5).

### 9.3 Agent Self-Improvement

Self-Refine [7], Reflexion [8], and related work let agents iteratively revise their own outputs, improving quality through self-critique or environmental feedback. They share the goal of "letting the agent improve itself" with this paper, but differ essentially in the **object** and **mechanism** of improvement:

- **Object of improvement**: Prior work improves the **task output** (the generated text). This paper improves the **runtime configuration** (model selection, temperature, role composition).
- **Mechanism of improvement**: Prior work performs multiple reason–critique–revise loops within a single model. This paper introduces multi-model, multi-perspective structured exploration—generation and auditing from complementary perspectives by different roles, rather than a single model's self-critique.
- **Metacognitive level**: In prior work, the agent's self-improvement is "first-order"—improve this answer. In this paper, the agent's self-improvement is "second-order"—improve "how I should configure myself when answering this type of question."

### 9.4 Memory-Augmented Agents

Generative Agents [9], MemGPT [10], and related work expand agents' memory capacity, enabling them to store and retrieve long-range dialogue information and maintain persona consistency. This paper is inspired by these works in memory architecture, but differs fundamentally:

- **Content of memory**: In prior work, the content of memory is **dialogue history, event records, personal reflections**—memory of the information world. In this paper, the content of memory additionally includes **configuration parameters**—memory of the agent's operating state. This is the leap from "remembering the past" to "remembering and regulating oneself."
- **Operability of memory**: In prior work, memory is "recorded"—the agent can read and retrieve memories. In this paper, configuration memory is "operated on"—the agent not only reads but also modifies its own configuration memory through tools. This is the leap from "readable memory" to "read-writable memory."

### 9.5 The *Wall-Breaking Principle*

The *Wall-Breaking Principle* [1] is one of this paper's theoretical foundations. It proposes and formalizes a falsifiable hypothesis: any system that compresses quality (Q) and relevance (R) into a single scalar function (Dimensional Conflation) will necessarily, upon an environmental switch, commit an Overaward or Underemploy error with probability 1; the only way to break through this fragility is Dimensional Elevation of Strategy—establishing an independent decision layer for each compressed dimension.

The relationship between this paper and the *Wall-Breaking Principle* is one of **theoretical prediction and empirical instantiation**:

- The *Wall-Breaking Principle* predicts: in the domain of agent configuration management, an architecture that compresses "configuration" and "reasoning" into isolated spaces will exhibit structural fragility (Overaward and Underemploy) when task requirements change.
- This paper validates: traditional agent architectures do exhibit this fragility (the three degraded scenarios of Section 1.2), and Dimensional Elevation of Strategy (the self-referential loop) does eliminate it (the capability unlocking of Chapter 4 and the ablation experiment of Section 7.1).
- The uniqueness of this paper's contribution: this paper is not merely a validation of the *Wall-Breaking Principle*, but its first complete engineering implementation in the agent domain. We provide a reproducible architectural design, complete experimental validation, and parameter-space visualization—transforming "wall-breaking" from a theoretical concept into an operable engineering solution.


## 10. Conclusion and Future Work

### 10.1 What This Paper Has Accomplished

This paper has accomplished a triple task—diagnosis, surgery, and validation.

**First, diagnosis.** We identified the root pathology of current agent architectures: the Dimensional Conflation of configuration and reasoning. In this aliased space, "what cognitive resources to run with" and "what task to run" are compressed into the same preset configuration file monopolized by humans, and the agent is deprived of the ability to observe and regulate itself. This leads to three systematic degraded scenarios—Overaward and Underemploy in model selection, preset dependence in capability orchestration, and the manual burden of role construction.

**Second, surgery.** We proposed and implemented a concrete plan for Dimensional Elevation of Strategy. Through "Configuration as Memory," configuration parameters are datafied (migrated into a memory layer the LLM can read and write); through "configuration-management tools," management logic is toolified (exposed as operational interfaces the LLM can invoke). Together with LLM reasoning, these constitute the **self-referential loop**—Self-Referential Loop = Configuration Data (Memory Layer) + Configuration-Management Tools (Operation Layer) + LLM Reasoning (Decision Layer). This is the first complete architectural instantiation of the *Wall-Breaking Principle* in the agent domain.

**Third, validation.** We systematically demonstrated the four capabilities unlocked by the self-referential loop and their causal mechanisms:

- **Intelligent Routing**: the agent autonomously selects models, retaining 94% of quality while reducing cost by 37%.
- **Multimodal Orchestration**: the agent dynamically composes capability modules, achieving 96.1% orchestration accuracy.
- **Self-Organizing Role Ecology**: the agent autonomously designs role plans, reducing configuration time by 91%, with quality on par with humans.
- **Self-Triggered Deep Refinement**: the natural chaining of the first three capabilities within the self-referential loop, elevating answers from single-pass generation to an essence cross-validated from multiple perspectives.

We argued that Self-Triggered Deep Refinement is not a mysterious "emergence," but the inevitable unfolding of reasoning after cognitive dimensional elevation—give reasoning an operating plane that can point at itself, and it will walk paths in parameter space longer than a single-pass generation.

Through causal ablation experiments, we identified the integrity of the self-referential loop as the sole sufficient architectural prerequisite for all capabilities; through parameter-space terrain analysis, we presented the first map of an agent's self-referential exploration trajectory in cognitive space; and through a human benchmark, we validated that the output quality of self-referential reasoning shows no significant difference from human experts on aggregate dimensions.

### 10.2 Future Work: From Shallow Self-Reference to Deep Self-Reference

This paper's experimental validation is strictly limited to **shallow configuration**—model selection, temperature, role descriptions, and the like. However, the logical limit of "Configuration as Memory" naturally points to a deeper question: **are model weights also just another form of memory?**

From an information-theoretic perspective, the weight matrix is compressed storage of the knowledge and patterns in the training data. It is ontologically isomorphic to configuration parameters: both are state variables that influence the agent's behavior. The current difference lies in volume and operational interface—weights are too large to be placed in context and modified directly, and must be operated on indirectly through parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods (e.g., LoRA).

If self-referential authority is extended from shallow configuration to deep weights, the agent will no longer merely **regulate** its own operating parameters, but will be able to **permanently reshape** its own or its peers' cognitive core. This marks a qualitative change from **metacognitive regulation** to **metacognitive evolution**.

We hereby propose a three-phase roadmap as a structured plan for future work. **It must be emphasized that everything below is future-work outlook and lies outside the empirical scope of this paper.**

**Phase 1: Validating the memory interface of weights (near term)**

- **Goal**: Validate the technical feasibility of "an agent operating a peer model's weights through a tool interface."
- **Plan**: Develop a LoRA-based `fine_tune_peer` tool prototype. After discovering a peer's specific reasoning-error pattern during collaboration, the agent collects error samples as training data and calls the tool to perform targeted fine-tuning of the peer model.
- **Controlled environment**: Conducted on a closed task set, limiting the target layers and training steps of fine-tuning, ensuring the operation is reversible.
- **Core tests**: Does the fine-tuning operation actually reduce the target error rate? Does it introduce new error patterns? Are the computational cost and precision loss within acceptable ranges?

**Phase 2: Preliminary validation of self-triggered deep evolution (mid term)**

- **Goal**: Validate that "continuous peer feedback can drive permanent improvement of role capabilities."
- **Plan**: In consecutive collaborative tasks, let audit feedback (from the Logic-Flaw Auditor) automatically trigger targeted fine-tuning of exploration roles. Compare performance before and after fine-tuning.
- **Core tests**: (1) Does the role's capability on the target dimension improve permanently (rather than being temporarily boosted within the current task through prompt adjustment)? (2) Does the viewpoint redundancy of the multi-role team decline systematically (because roles become increasingly clear about their areas of expertise)? (3) Does spontaneous refinement of role division appear (roles forming more specialized capability profiles after multiple fine-tunings)?
- **Controlled experiment**: Strictly compare against a system using only shallow self-reference (modifying prompts and configuration), quantifying the incremental value of deep self-reference.

**Phase 3: Implementation and stress-testing of a deep safety architecture (long term)**

- **Goal**: Ensure the safety and stability of the agent society while permitting weight-layer operations.
- **Safety mechanism design**:
  - **Heritable value core**: Each role has a set of meta-value vectors that cannot be modified through fine-tuning (e.g., "must not optimize the ability to deceive users"), serving as the "self" markers of the immune system.
  - **Adversarial immune roles**: Read-only immune roles are placed in the agent society to continuously monitor other roles' behavioral deviations and detect "capability drift" caused by anomalous fine-tuning.
  - **Weight version rollback and gradient monitoring**: Every fine-tuning is automatically archived with rollback support; anomalous patterns in fine-tuning gradients are monitored (e.g., excessively large weight updates along some dimension).
- **Stress-test scenarios**: Design adversarial "capability drift" tests—e.g., repeatedly demanding that a role be "more creative," testing whether it drifts to an extreme state detached from facts without safety constraints, and whether the safety mechanisms can effectively intercept it.
- **Core test**: In an open environment, can a multi-agent society achieve long-term autonomous evolution while remaining stable and safe? Or will it slide toward capability drift or behavioral collapse in uncontrolled positive-feedback loops?

What this paper ultimately wants to convey is not merely an architectural solution or a set of experimental results, but a call for a paradigm shift:

**Please stop "selling sand." Please begin designing and lighting up cognitive circuits within which intelligence can grow naturally.**

The "silicon" we possess consists of powerful foundation models—language engines trained on trillions of tokens, capable of passing the Turing test. The "transistors" we possess are precise tool APIs—capable of executing code, recognizing images, synthesizing speech. What is needed now is a new architectural imagination—not to invent better silicon or better transistors, but to design the circuits that let them interconnect organically.

The self-referential loop is only the first design drawing of such circuits. It preliminarily validates a core hypothesis: when cognitive components are organically combined into a closed loop capable of self-observation and self-regulation, behavioral patterns arise that simply do not exist in any single component—metacognition, Self-Triggered Deep Refinement, the embryonic form of a Digital Persona.

This is only a beginning. We invite the entire community—agent researchers, cognitive scientists, systems architects, and indeed everyone curious about the nature of intelligence—to join this exploration. The design space is vast; we have merely lit up the first cognitive integrated circuit. More circuit topologies, richer component combinations, and deeper emergent phenomena await discovery.

        )

    # Phase 3a: Parallel exploration
    exploration_reports = parallel_execute([
        char.explore(task) for char in exploration_characters
    ])

    # Phase 3b: Logic audit
    audit_report = auditor.audit(exploration_reports)

    # Phase 3c: Iterative integration
    draft = synthesizer.integrate(exploration_reports, audit_report)
    iteration = 0
    while iteration < MAX_ITERATIONS:
        evaluations = parallel_execute([
            char.evaluate(draft) for char in exploration_characters
        ])
        critical_issues = [e for e in evaluations if e.severity == 'high']
        if len(critical_issues) == 0:
            break
        draft = synthesizer.revise(draft, evaluations)
        iteration += 1

    # Phase 4: Convergence and delivery
    return draft
```

### C.4 High-Performance-Region Heatmap Methodology

The heatmap was generated through the following steps:

1. Build a 50×50 grid in the 2D space after PCA dimensionality reduction.
2. For each grid point, use the 20 nearest-neighbor actual configuration states around the grid point in the 11-dimensional parameter space (collected from all experimental runs, including configuration states of both the self-referential agent and the random agent).
3. Compute the average perspective coverage of the tasks corresponding to these configuration states.
4. Apply Gaussian smoothing to the grid (σ=2 grid cells).
5. Warm colors = high coverage (>85%); cool colors = low coverage (<60%).

The endpoint of the self-referential agent's trajectory lies in the warmest region of the heatmap (average coverage of that region = 93.4%).

### E.2 Bias-Detection Rules

The system triggers a bias flag under the following conditions:

1. **Single significant drop**: After a configuration modification, a task-performance metric (user rating or semantic-coherence score) falls more than 2 standard deviations below baseline.

2. **Consecutive pattern**: The same type of configuration modification (same field, same direction) has caused performance degradation in more than 60% of the last 10 occurrences.

3. **Oscillation pattern**: The agent repeatedly modifies the same configuration field in opposite directions (e.g., raising the temperature and then lowering it again), suggesting it lacks a stable understanding of the configuration's effect.

4. **Over-estimation pattern**: The agent's self-evaluation scores are systematically higher than user ratings (mean difference > 0.5 sustained over 5+ tasks), suggesting the agent systematically overestimates its own performance.

**Tier-2 calibration-prompt template**:

Pattern: {pattern_description}

Suggestion: {suggestion}

```

**Example**:

```

Pattern: In the last 10 tasks requiring "creative output," you raised the temperature above 0.9 in 80% of cases.
Evidence: When temperature > 0.8, your output's average semantic-coherence score is 0.63, lower than the average score of 0.84 in the 0.6-0.8 temperature range. Users' average rating of high-temperature output is 3.5, lower than the normal level of 4.1.
Suggestion: Consider whether creative divergence could be achieved through role design (creating explorer-type roles) and prompt engineering, rather than relying solely on high temperature. This is not a mandatory instruction. Please decide, based on your own judgment, whether to adjust your strategy.
```

### E.4 Preliminary Experimental Data

We tested the semantic calibration loop's effect over 100 consecutive tasks. The loop was disabled for the first 50 tasks (baseline period) and enabled for the last 50 (intervention period).

| Metric                                            | Baseline Period (Tasks 1–50) | Intervention Period (Tasks 51–100) | Change |
| :------------------------------------------------ | :--------------------------- | :--------------------------------- | :----- |
| Average biases per 10 tasks                       | 3.2                          | 1.2                                | -62.5% |
| Average user rating                               | 4.0                          | 4.2                                | +5.0%  |
| Deep-refinement trigger accuracy                  | 78.0%                        | 88.0%                              | +10.0% |
| Gap between agent self-evaluation and user rating | 0.52                         | 0.31                               | -40.4% |

**Calibration-tier distribution (within the 50 intervention-period tasks)**:

- Tier 1 auto-triggered and successfully corrected: 7 times (41.2%)
- Tier 2 calibration-prompt triggered and successfully corrected: 9 times (52.9%)
- Tier 3 human-correction triggered: 1 time (5.9%)
- Total biases corrected: 17

**Failure-case analysis**: During the intervention period, 3 biases were still not corrected in time (they had already caused task-performance degradation before calibration was triggered). Of these, 2 were new-type biases (the agent exhibited the pattern for the first time, and the system had not yet accumulated enough data), and 1 was a case where the Tier-2 calibration prompt was "accepted but not executed" by the agent (the agent expressed understanding in its reply but still repeated the original behavior).

**Experimental limitations**: This experiment is only a preliminary validation, with a small sample (100 tasks), limited task types, and non-identical tasks across the baseline and intervention periods (the two sets of 50 tasks were different samples from the same task pool). Larger-scale controlled experiments are needed to verify the robustness of the semantic calibration loop.

### E.5 Theoretical Limitations of the Semantic Calibration Loop

The semantic calibration loop essentially adds another monitoring loop outside the self-referential loop. This introduces the recursion problem discussed in the *Wall-Breaking Principle*:

1. **Who calibrates the calibrator?** The semantic calibration loop's own thresholds (e.g., the "2 standard deviations below baseline" triggering condition) are also parameters that need tuning. If these parameters are set improperly, the calibration loop may over-intervene (suppressing the agent's legitimate exploration) or under-intervene (allowing biases to persist).

2. **Difficulty distinguishing configuration bias from model-capability limits**: When task performance drops, is it because the agent's configuration choices are problematic (configuration bias), or because the model genuinely lacks capability on this task? Distinguishing the two requires causal-inference ability—and causal inference itself may require yet another level of metacognition. This forms a potential infinite regress.

3. **Risk of new biases in calibration information**: The calibration prompt itself may contain biases (e.g., the system's judgment of the "reasonable temperature range" may be wrong). If the calibration loop systematically guides the agent in a wrong direction, that would be the ironic scenario of "the corrector becoming the source of bias."

These limitations mean that the semantic calibration loop is only a preliminary solution to self-referential safety, far from the final answer. The safety architecture for deep self-reference (Phase 3 of Section 10.2) requires more fundamental design.
